\section*{Summary}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Summary}

Transposable elements (TEs) are mobile DNA sequences that occupy a substantial fraction of every vertebrate genome. Their accurate annotation underpins comparative genomics and the study of genome evolution, but existing tools rely on alignment to curated reference libraries and on manual curation, which scale poorly with the volume of data now produced by long-read assembly projects and degrade on divergent or lineage-specific families. This project develops deep-learning classifiers trained directly on the Pantera catalogue of approximately 145{,}000 TE consensi from the Vertebrate Genomes Project (VGP), and asks how far convolutional and graph-based representations of raw DNA sequence can replace alignment as the basis for TE classification.

Four models are presented in sequence. A reverse-complement-invariant tower CNN with multi-scale and dilated convolutions is first trained for terminal-inverted-repeat (TIR) detection, where careful subsampling outperforms loss-based reweightings. A hierarchical CNN classifier (v3) extends this encoder with joint binary (DNA vs non-DNA) and seven-way DNA-superfamily heads, reaching 91.3\% binary accuracy and 94.4\% superfamily accuracy on a held-out test set. A hybrid CNN+GNN model (v4) adds a parallel windowed canonical-7-mer graph branch fused to the CNN through a learned attention gate; the gate exposes a task-dependent split between local motifs and global composition. Broadening the head to three top-level classes and 23 superfamilies (v4.3), with three species withheld for external benchmarking, gives 98.2\% three-class accuracy (macro $F_1 = 0.981$) and 89.3\% superfamily accuracy ($F_1 = 0.839$).

External evaluation on the three held-out species (zebra finch, platypus, American alligator) shows lineage-dependent domain shift: top-level transfer is reasonable on platypus (73.8\%) and alligator (60.1\%) but collapses on zebra finch (33.3\%), driven almost entirely by avian LTR elements. Gradient-based saliency analysis confirms that the model concentrates evidence at biologically canonical regions (TIR boundaries for DNA elements, poly(A) tracts for LINEs), and unsupervised contrastive learning on the same encoder recovers the top-level class structure without labels and reveals candidate subclusters within DNA superfamilies that warrant curation.

Two cross-cutting findings emerge. First, top-level class discrimination is intrinsically easier and more robust than fine-grained superfamily assignment: superfamily heads overfit substantially more than class heads, transfer worse across lineages, and benefit much less from the GNN branch. Second, the boundary-prediction failure observed throughout v1--v4.3 (length is recovered at $r \approx 0.94$ but absolute canvas position is not, $r \approx 0.07$--$0.18$) is structural rather than statistical: the regression head reads from a position-invariant pooled embedding. An architectural fix --- sinusoidal positional channels and a per-position segmentation head --- is sketched as the natural next iteration of the model.

\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Transposable Elements}

% AUDIT(L,C) [L1,C4]: narrowed vertebrate-fraction claim to human; added
% qualifier for other vertebrates.
Transposable elements (TEs) are mobile DNA sequences that propagate copies of themselves within their host genome \cite{wells2020field}. Eukaryotic genomes are extensively colonised by TEs: they constitute roughly half of the human genome, with fractions ranging from under 10\% to over 80\% across other vertebrates \cite{wells2020field}, and contribute to chromosomal architecture, gene regulation and species-specific adaptation. Some TE families are autonomous, encoding the enzymes (transposase or reverse transcriptase) required for their own mobilisation; others are non-autonomous, parasitising the machinery of an active autonomous driver \cite{wells2020field}.

Knowing which TE families are present in a newly assembled genome, and which copies belong to which family, is a prerequisite for almost every downstream genomic analysis: variant interpretation, comparative genomics, and the study of TE--host coevolution all rely on accurate TE annotation. Existing tools rely on alignment to curated reference libraries together with hand-tuned feature detection and manual curation \cite{smit2015repeatmasker}. These approaches scale poorly with the volume of data now produced by long-read assembly projects, and they perform especially badly on divergent or novel families and on non-autonomous elements that are difficult to assign to a driver. A learned classifier that can be trained directly from large catalogues of unannotated insertions would therefore be a valuable complement to alignment-based pipelines.

\subsection{Sequence Features of TE Classes}

The classification task is structured by the biology of how each TE class moves \cite{wells2020field}. DNA transposons (Class~II) move by a cut-and-paste mechanism, with autonomous members encoding a transposase that recognises terminal inverted repeats (TIRs) at the boundaries of the element. TIRs are typically 10--50~bp \cite{wells2020field}, near-perfect reverse complements of one another, forming a structural signature that a sequence classifier can exploit locally. Class~I elements move via an RNA intermediate. LTR retrotransposons are flanked by direct long terminal repeats (typically 200--600~bp) \cite{wells2020field} carrying the regulatory elements for transcription, and internally encode a polyprotein with reverse transcriptase and integrase domains. Long interspersed nuclear elements (LINEs) lack LTRs and instead end with a poly(A) tail; they replicate by target-primed reverse transcription and the autonomous members encode an endonuclease together with a reverse transcriptase.

Two structural features of TE sequences directly constrain the architecture of any classifier. First, a TE may be sequenced from either strand, so a classifier should produce the same prediction for a sequence and its reverse complement (RC). This RC symmetry is a hard constraint that is most cleanly imposed inside the model rather than via test-time augmentation. Second, a TIR is by definition a reverse-complement match between the two ends of the element; detecting it requires comparing positions that may be several kilobases apart in a sequence whose total length varies by two orders of magnitude across families.

\subsection{Previous Work and the Pantera Catalogue}

The most widely used tool for TE annotation, RepeatMasker \cite{smit2015repeatmasker}, identifies repeats by aligning a query genome against a curated library such as Dfam or Repbase. Its output is therefore only as good as the underlying library: divergent or lineage-specific families are missed or systematically misclassified. Several recent efforts have applied machine learning to TE detection or sub-classification; however, most have either focused on a single TE class or been trained on the same curated reference libraries they are meant to extend, limiting their ability to generalise to novel or lineage-specific families.

The starting point for this project is the Pantera catalogue, which uses pangenome polymorphisms to detect TE insertions that are absent in some haplotypes of a species \cite{sierra2024pantera}. Applied to the Vertebrate Genomes Project (VGP) assemblies, Pantera produces a catalogue of approximately 145{,}000 sequences from over fifty vertebrate species, with FASTA-header annotations giving the broad TE class (DNA, LTR, LINE, SINE, RC, \dots) and, where assignable, the superfamily. Because Pantera detects elements directly from polymorphism, it provides an unusually large, alignment-independent training set on which a learned classifier can be built without circularly inheriting the biases of a reference library. The dataset is, however, heavily skewed: roughly 92\% of sequences carry no transposase annotation, DNA transposons constitute only about 8\%, and many superfamilies have fewer than a hundred examples.

\subsection{Convolutional Neural Networks for Sequence Classification}

Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) \cite{lecun2015deep,krizhevsky2012imagenet} are a natural choice for detecting short, position-invariant motifs in biological sequences. A 1-D convolution over a one-hot-encoded sequence learns a bank of filters whose activations indicate where in the sequence each motif occurs; stacking convolutions with non-linearities then composes these motifs into longer patterns. Two architectural ingredients matter for TE sequences in particular. \emph{Multi-scale} convolutions, with several kernel widths in parallel, allow the network to capture both short, locally conserved motifs and longer structural features in the same forward pass. \emph{Dilated} convolutions enlarge the receptive field exponentially with depth without an equivalent growth in parameters, which is necessary to span TIRs whose two halves may be several kilobases apart.

CNNs also have well-known limitations. After global pooling, all positional information is averaged out, so a CNN-only classifier cannot directly use absolute or relative position within the sequence. Within the receptive field of a given filter, the precise spatial layout of nucleotides is preserved, but anything more global must be re-learned from indirect cues such as motif density.

\subsection{Graph Neural Networks for Compositional Information}

% RF = 0.848 ± 0.015. Both intro and caption now read ~85%, which is correct.
A complementary view of a TE sequence is its $k$-mer composition: the multiset of all length-$k$ substrings, which captures global compositional bias and many long-range correlations that motif filters do not see. Even a logistic regression on canonical 7-mer frequencies separates the three top-level classes (DNA, LTR, LINE) at roughly 85\% macro $F_1$ (Section~\ref{ssec:fullset}), establishing that compositional signal alone is highly informative for class-level identification. However, a single global $k$-mer histogram discards positional structure entirely, and a fixed positional binning would tie the representation to a particular sequence length.

A graph neural network (GNN) \cite{kipf2016semi,hamilton2017inductive,zhou2019graph} provides a flexible compromise. The sequence is broken into overlapping windows; each window contributes a node whose feature vector is its windowed $k$-mer profile, and adjacent windows are connected by edges. Message passing then mixes information between neighbouring windows over several layers, producing a global representation that retains coarse positional structure (which compositional pattern appears where along the sequence) without requiring the input to be padded to a fixed length. Using \emph{canonical} $k$-mers (the lexicographically smaller of a $k$-mer and its reverse complement) keeps the representation strand-symmetric, in line with the RC invariance discussed above.

\subsection{Attention Fusion and Hierarchical Heads}

CNN and GNN branches see complementary aspects of the same sequence: the CNN sees local motifs, the GNN sees windowed composition. There is no \emph{a priori} reason to prefer a fixed weighting of the two; some sequences may be classifiable from a single sharp motif and others from broad compositional shifts. An attention-style gating layer learns a per-input convex combination of the two branch embeddings, so that the fused representation can lean on whichever modality is locally more informative.

The downstream classifier is itself hierarchical. A binary head distinguishes DNA transposons from all other TEs (or, in later versions, separates the three top-level classes DNA, LTR and LINE), while a superfamily head provides the finer assignment within each top-level class. This mirrors both the biological hierarchy of TE classification and the empirical observation, returned to throughout the Results, that top-level discrimination is intrinsically easier and more robust than fine-grained superfamily assignment.

This thesis develops these ideas through a sequence of progressively more capable models. After describing the data and architectures (Section~\ref{sec:methods}), I evaluate a TIR-detection CNN as a proof of concept, a hierarchical CNN classifier (v3) for DNA superfamilies, a hybrid CNN+GNN model (v4) that adds compositional signal via a $k$-mer graph, and a three-class extension (v4.3) that is also evaluated on three held-out species as an external benchmark (Section~\ref{sec:results}). Two interpretability analyses --- gradient-based saliency and unsupervised contrastive clustering --- close the Results before the Discussion.

\section{Data and Methods}\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Data}\label{ssec:data}

The training data are transposable element sequences identified by Pantera \cite{sierra2024pantera} from the Vertebrate Genomes Project (VGP) assemblies. Pantera detects TE families directly from pangenome polymorphisms by identifying insertions present in some haplotypes and absent in others, and so produces a comprehensive catalogue of recently active TEs without inheriting the family list of any particular reference library. The catalogue used here contains approximately 145{,}000 sequences from over fifty vertebrate species.

Two annotation sets are used:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{TIR labels}, derived from a structural search for terminal inverted repeats at the boundaries of each Pantera consensus. These give a binary label (TIR present / absent) and are used for the v1 TIR-detection model.
    \item \textbf{Class labels}, parsed from the RepeatMasker-style FASTA headers of each consensus. These give the top-level class (DNA / LTR / LINE / SINE / RC / unannotated) and, where available, the superfamily (e.g.\ DNA/hAT, LTR/Gypsy, LINE/CR1).
\end{itemize}

The class distribution is heavily imbalanced: approximately 92\% of sequences carry no transposase annotation, DNA transposons constitute roughly 8\%, and within DNA only seven superfamilies (DNA/hAT, TcMar-Tc1, DNA/PIF-Harbinger, DNA/CMC, DNA/MULE, DNA/Kolobok, DNA/PiggyBac) have at least 100 examples. Sequence length varies over two orders of magnitude (173~bp to 19{,}907~bp; median 3{,}263~bp). Figures~\ref{fig:class_distribution} and~\ref{fig:superfamily_distribution} summarise the class and superfamily distributions.

\subsection{Sequence Representation}\label{ssec:repr}

All models take one-hot encoded DNA sequences as input. Each nucleotide is mapped to one of five channels (A, C, G, T, N), where N covers ambiguous or non-standard bases; channels are stacked along the feature dimension to produce a tensor of shape (5, $L$).

Because CNNs require a fixed input length, every sequence is placed on a fixed-length canvas of $L = 40$~kb. The placement is randomised per epoch: for a sequence of length $\ell$, a start position $s$ is sampled uniformly from $\{0, 1, \dots, L-\ell\}$, the sequence occupies positions $s$ through $s+\ell-1$, and the rest of the canvas is filled with zero columns. A binary mask records which positions carry real sequence; the mask is consumed by the masked global pooling layer so that padded positions do not contribute to the pooled embedding. Random placement during training also serves as positional augmentation: the model sees each sequence at many different absolute offsets across epochs.

Reverse-complement invariance is built into the encoding. The reverse complement of a one-hot tensor is obtained by reversing the spatial axis and remapping channels (A$\leftrightarrow$T, C$\leftrightarrow$G); this is a deterministic, on-the-fly operation that adds no new parameters. Both the forward and the reverse-complement views of a sequence are passed through the encoder during the forward pass (Section~\ref{ssec:methods_v3}).

\subsection{Model v1 — TIR Detection CNN}\label{ssec:methods_v1}

The v1 model is a single-task binary classifier for TIR presence. The encoder is a multi-scale tower CNN: three parallel convolutional branches with kernel widths 7, 15 and 21~bp run on the input one-hot tensor, each producing 128 feature maps. The three branches are concatenated along the channel dimension and mixed by a $1\!\times\!1$ convolution back to 128 channels. Four dilated convolutional blocks with kernel size 9 and dilation rates $\{1, 2, 4, 8\}$ then expand the receptive field, each block using GELU activation, batch normalisation, dropout (0.15) and a residual connection. A masked global average pool produces a 128-dimensional sequence embedding, which is fed to a small binary classification head.

Reverse-complement invariance is achieved by late fusion: the same encoder is run on the forward sequence and on its reverse complement, and the two resulting embeddings are averaged before the classification head. An earlier variant fused immediately after the first convolutional layer, but late fusion proved both simpler and more accurate (Section~\ref{ssec:hybrid_disc}).

\subsection{Model v3 — Hierarchical CNN Classifier}\label{ssec:methods_v3}

The v3 classifier uses the same tower CNN encoder as v1, but feeds the pooled embedding into two parallel heads: a binary head that distinguishes DNA transposons from all other TE classes, and a seven-way superfamily head over the DNA superfamilies with at least 100 training examples. Both heads are trained jointly, with the superfamily loss masked out for non-DNA samples so that only DNA examples contribute to the superfamily gradient. This hierarchical design reflects the biological organisation of the labels: DNA transposons share structural features that separate them from retrotransposons, and within DNA, finer motifs distinguish superfamilies.

The class imbalance described in Section~\ref{ssec:data} is addressed at two levels. The non-DNA majority class is subsampled from approximately 135{,}000 to 20{,}000 sequences before training, which keeps enough negatives to learn a reliable binary boundary while preventing the model from collapsing to a constant prediction. Within DNA, superfamilies with fewer than 100 examples are excluded from the multi-class head, leaving the seven superfamilies above. The remaining superfamily imbalance is handled by inverse square-root class weighting in the superfamily loss. After this filtering and balancing, the v3 training set contains 20{,}000 non-DNA sequences and 30{,}431 DNA sequences across seven superfamilies, with 6{,}141 sequences held out for testing.

\subsection{Model v4 — Hybrid CNN + k-mer GNN}\label{ssec:methods_v4}

The v4 hybrid model adds a parallel $k$-mer graph neural network branch to the v3 encoder. The CNN branch is unchanged; the GNN branch operates on a sequence-level graph constructed as follows. The sequence is divided into 512~bp sliding windows with stride 256~bp (50\% overlap). For each window, all $k=7$ subsequences are extracted and counted using \emph{canonical} $k$-mers (the lexicographically smaller of the $k$-mer and its reverse complement) so that the representation is strand-symmetric, then hashed into a fixed-size feature vector of dimension 2048. A short relative positional encoding is concatenated to each window's feature vector to keep coarse positional information. The windows form the nodes of a chain graph in which adjacent windows are connected by bidirectional edges with self-loops. A 3-layer GraphSAGE-style \cite{hamilton2017inductive} message-passing GNN with hidden dimension 128 then aggregates information across neighbouring windows, and a scatter mean pool produces a single 128-dimensional graph-level embedding.

The CNN and GNN embeddings are combined by an attention-style gate. A small MLP takes the concatenation of the two embeddings as input and outputs a two-element softmax $(g_{\mathrm{CNN}}, g_{\mathrm{GNN}})$; the fused embedding is the convex combination $g_{\mathrm{CNN}} \cdot e_{\mathrm{CNN}} + g_{\mathrm{GNN}} \cdot e_{\mathrm{GNN}}$. The fused embedding is then fed to the same hierarchical heads as in v3.

The same architecture is used for the three-class extensions v4.2 and v4.3, with only the top-level head broadened from binary to three-way (DNA / LTR / LINE) and the superfamily head broadened from 7 to 23 outputs. v4.2 is trained on the full VGP set; v4.3 additionally excludes the three benchmark species (Section~\ref{ssec:data}) and caps training at 40 epochs to mitigate the overfitting observed in v4.2 (Section~\ref{ssec:fullset}).

\subsection{Training}\label{ssec:training}

All models are trained with the AdamW optimiser \cite{loshchilov2019decoupled} ($\mathrm{lr} = 10^{-3}$, weight decay $10^{-4}$) and a cosine annealing schedule. Class imbalance is addressed at two levels, as described above: subsampling for the binary task, and inverse square-root class weighting for the superfamily task. Multi-class heads use label smoothing ($\alpha = 0.1$).

\subsection{Evaluation}\label{ssec:evaluation}

Model performance is reported as accuracy, balanced accuracy, macro and weighted $F_1$, and per-class $F_1$. AUROC and AUPRC are reported for binary tasks. For hierarchical models I separately report top-level (DNA vs non-DNA, or three-class) and superfamily-level metrics, and confusion matrices at both levels. Per-superfamily $F_1$ is reported only for superfamilies with at least 30 test samples to avoid spurious significance from very rare classes.

For the external benchmark (Section~\ref{ssec:mini_benchmark}), evaluation is performed directly on the curated TE libraries of the three held-out species. Each FASTA header is parsed for top-level class and superfamily, and labels are mapped to the model's vocabulary using a hierarchical strategy: exact superfamily match where available, parent-class fallback (e.g.\ DNA/X $\to$ DNA) when the specific subfamily is unseen, and exclusion from superfamily scoring otherwise. Sequences whose top-level class is outside the model's three-way head (e.g.\ tRNA, SINE, RC/Helitron, Unknown) are excluded from the evaluable set.

\subsection{Terminal Inverted Repeat Detection}

Terminal inverted repeats (TIRs) are a defining structural feature of DNA transposons, consisting of near-identical sequences in opposite orientations at the element boundaries. Detecting TIR presence is a fundamental task for TE classification, as it distinguishes DNA transposons from other TE classes such as LTR retrotransposons and LINEs. As an introduction to the later deep learning tasks, I started with developing a convolutional neural network (CNN) specifically designed for binary TIR detection, addressing two key challenges: the inherent reverse-complement symmetry of TIRs and severe class imbalance in the training data.

\subsubsection{Handling Class Imbalance}

The TIR-detection dataset is moderately imbalanced: of the 145{,}000 Pantera sequences, approximately 11{,}500 carry a TIR annotation, giving a positive rate of roughly 8\% before subsampling; after stratified subsampling to a balanced training split the effective positive fraction is 50\%. I compared inverse frequency class weighting, inverse square-root weighting, focal loss \cite{lin2017focal}, and stratified subsampling. The simplest of these --- selecting a balanced subset of the data --- consistently outperformed the loss-based reweightings, both in macro $F_1$ and in training stability.

\subsection{Multi-Class Extension: Three-Class Classification (v4.2 and v4.3)}\label{ssec:fullset}

The binary models described in Sections~\ref{ssec:hier} and~\ref{ssec:hybrid} classify DNA transposons against all other TEs, which are being placed in a single non-DNA category. This formulation discards the finer-grained supervision available in the dataset. I extended the hybrid CNN+GNN model to a three-class head distinguishing DNA transposons, LTR retrotransposons, and LINEs, developing two successive versions: v4.2, trained on the full VGP dataset, and v4.3, which reserves three benchmark species for external validation.

\subsubsection{Three-Class Reformulation}

The three-class head asks the model to distinguish: cut-and-paste DNA transposons; LTR retrotransposons, which copy via an RNA intermediate flanked by long terminal repeats; and LINEs, which replicate through target-primed reverse transcription. Sequences with ambiguous or rare class labels (SINE, PLE, RC, and unannotated non-DNA entries) are excluded from training and evaluation due to severe class imbalance. After filtering, 50,431 sequences remain distributed across 23 superfamilies. To further balance representation, each superfamily's training contribution is capped at 3,000 sequences, so the effective training set contains 30,727 sequences. The CNN and GNN tower architectures are unchanged from the hybrid model (Section~\ref{ssec:hybrid}); only the top-level classification head is broadened from two outputs to three, with superfamily subheads spanning all three major classes.

\subsubsection{Overfitting Analysis in v4.2}

The first three-class version (v4.2) was trained on the full VGP dataset for up to 65 epochs. However, the result is concerning: both training and validation superfamily F1 reaching 0.999, while the held-out test set's superfamily macro F1 dropped to 0.872. The gap of 0.127 shows a clear signal of moderate overfitting. On the other hand, the F1 score for the top-level three-class head generalized much better (test F1 = 0.984, gap = 0.016), indicating that three-class discrimination is inherently robust while fine-grained superfamily classification is susceptible to memorizing genome-specific sequence patterns. 

This overfitting pattern emerged despite inverse square-root class weighting and early stopping on validation macro $F_1$, consistent with a scenario in which the model fits genome-specific subvariants of rare superfamilies during training. This may be induced by the class imbalance and the presence of rare superfamilies. Especially for superfamilies with fewer than 500 training samples, the model can memorize genome-specific subvariants rather than learning generalizable features. Analysis of prediction confidence revealed that on test samples the model maintained high confidence even for misclassifications, indicating overconfident decision boundaries rather than principled uncertainty.

\subsection{Saliency-Based Attribution Analysis}\label{ssec:saliency}

To understand which sequence regions drive model predictions, I computed input-level gradients (saliency maps) \cite{simonyan2014deep} using backpropagation of the logit vector through the network to the input. For each test sequence, I computed $\nabla_x L$ where $L$ is the cross-entropy loss for the true class. This yields per-position attribution scores ranging from $-1$ (evidence against true class) to $+1$ (evidence for true class).

Aggregating across test sequences by superfamily, I identified that saliency concentrates sharply at distinct sequence boundaries rather than distributed uniformly across the interior. For DNA transposons, strong positive saliency clusters at sequence ends, consistent with TIR detection from Section~\ref{ssec:tir}. For LINE elements, saliency concentrates around poly-A and sequence-structure motifs. For LTR elements, the model emphasizes boundary regions and internal symmetry patterns characteristic of retroviral organization.

Crucially, saliency patterns vary predictably across misclassified vs.~correctly classified sequences: on DNA elements misclassified as LTR, saliency is weak or absent at TIR boundaries, consistent with either degraded TIR signals in those sequences or the model attending to alternative internal features. This diagnostic signal suggests that improving performance on rare superfamilies requires either augmentation to emphasize boundary regions, or adding explicit attention bottlenecks that force the model to attend to canonical TE features.

\subsection{Unsupervised Clustering of TE Sequences}\label{ssec:clustering}

In addition to supervised classification, I explored unsupervised learning to test whether the model's learned embeddings capture meaningful structure without class labels. This would test whether the feature space naturally stratifies along biologically meaningful axes, and may enable the discovery of novel TE subfamilies or structure not annotated in the training labels.

\subsubsection{Contrastive Learning Framework}

Initial experiments used supervised contrastive loss (SupCon) alone, which pulls embeddings from sequences of the same superfamily together while pushing apart those from different superfamilies. While this encourages well-clustered embeddings, a concern with pure SupCon is that it can push sequences apart in ways that are not biologically meaningful, particularly when negative pairs are sampled from superfamilies with broadly similar sequence composition. Early models required a hard-coded loss clipping to prevent excessive separation, which is not optimal and biologically arbitrary. 

The final implementation (v5) addresses this by combining three loss terms:
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{SupCon}} + \lambda_{\text{cls}} \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}} + \lambda_{\text{ctr}} \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{center}}
\]
The supervised contrastive loss $\mathcal{L}_{\text{SupCon}}$ encourages compact, well-separated embeddings; the cross-entropy classification loss $\mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}}$ from an auxiliary supervised head stabilizes the representation to true class labels, mitigating arbitrary pushing-apart; and the center loss $\mathcal{L}_{\text{center}}$ further tightens within-class clusters. This combined objective produces more biologically coherent embeddings than pure contrastive learning.

Clustering revealed subclusters within DNA transposons with unclear superfamily assignments, suggesting the presence of sequence groups that do not map cleanly to known families. These could represent sequence variants, degenerate copies, or artefactual joins in the reference annotations. Further validation against curated TE databases would be required to determine whether any represent genuinely novel subfamilies, highlighting the potential of unsupervised learning for TE curation and quality control.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{When Does the GNN Branch Help?}\label{ssec:hybrid_disc}

The hybrid CNN+GNN architecture was motivated by the hypothesis that local motif filters and global compositional features see complementary aspects of a TE sequence and that combining them via a learned attention gate would outperform either branch alone. The results bear this out only in part. The GNN contributes a clear improvement on the broad class-level task (DNA vs non-DNA in v4, three-way DNA/LTR/LINE in v4.3), where the gate assigns a higher average weight to the GNN branch on the binary task; in the three-class setting the gate is more variable and does not converge to a fixed ratio (Figure~\ref{fig:gate_weights}). Superfamily classification within DNA is essentially indistinguishable between the hybrid and the pure CNN of v3. This pattern is consistent with the supporting analysis in Section~\ref{ssec:fullset}: a logistic regression on canonical $k$-mer frequencies already recovers most of the top-level class structure, while within-class superfamily separation is poor. The mechanistic features that distinguish DNA transposons, LTR retrotransposons and LINEs --- and which are mirrored in their nucleotide-composition statistics --- are exactly what the GNN sees most clearly; the finer distinctions between superfamilies in the same class depend on local motifs that only the CNN can resolve. The attention gate is therefore best read as a soft prior on which modality to trust for a given input, not as a generic improvement on top of the CNN.

A second, more procedural lesson concerns reverse-complement invariance. An earlier variant of the encoder enforced RC symmetry by symmetrising the one-hot input and fusing immediately after the first convolutional layer. Late fusion --- running the CNN twice, once per strand, and averaging the resulting embeddings --- proved both simpler and at least as accurate. The likely reason is that early fusion forces the very first filters to be RC-symmetric, which restricts the family of representable motifs; late fusion allows each pass to learn strand-specific intermediate features that are only reconciled at the embedding level, while still guaranteeing exact RC invariance.

\subsection{Generalisation and Domain Shift}

The contrast between the within-VGP test set (v4.3 three-class accuracy 98.2\%) and the three held-out amniote species (overall 55.0\%, zebra finch 33.3\%) is the strongest evidence in this work that TE sequence features show substantial lineage-dependence, at least across the amniote clades sampled here. Within-class breakdowns localise the failure: DNA-class recall remains high across all three benchmark species, but the LTR class --- and within it the avian-specific ERV families --- carries almost all of the lost accuracy. Mammalian and reptilian LTR sequences in the training set evidently do not carry the features that distinguish avian LTR subfamilies, and a model trained without avian LTR examples therefore has no way to recover them.

This is a constraint on the training distribution rather than a deficiency of the architecture: the same encoder that fails on zebra finch LTR transfers reasonably well to platypus, whose TE landscape is closer to the mammalian and reptilian sequences seen during training. Two complementary remedies are natural. The first is simply broader phylogenetic coverage in the training set, with explicit stratification across vertebrate lineages so that each major branch contributes LTR examples. The second is architectural: domain-adversarial training~\cite{ganin2015unsupervised} and model-agnostic meta-learning~\cite{finn2017model} have been applied in other biological domains to learn representations that transfer across distribution shifts; analogous approaches could encourage the encoder to learn lineage-agnostic TE features, at the cost of additional training-time complexity.

\subsection{Overfitting Patterns Across the Hierarchy}

A consistent observation across v3, v4 and especially v4.2 is that superfamily heads overfit substantially more than top-level class heads on the same training run. In v4.2 the superfamily test gap reached 0.127 while the three-class gap was only 0.016. The asymmetry is again consistent with the broader picture: top-level classes are large, well-supported and biologically distinct, so their decision boundary is constrained by many examples; superfamilies are smaller, internally heterogeneous and partly defined by alignment to reference libraries, so the model has both the capacity and the incentive to memorise genome-specific subvariants.

The mitigations adopted in v4.3 --- early stopping at 40 epochs (just before the validation-loss inflection observed in v4.2), label smoothing, and explicit species hold-out --- reduced but did not eliminate the gap, and the residual gap is concentrated in the rarest superfamilies (e.g.\ DNA/CMC, LINE/L1-Tx1) where test support drops below 100 sequences. The most direct remedies are therefore at the data level: a higher minimum-sample threshold for inclusion, hierarchical evaluation that weights the top-level head more heavily for rare superfamilies, or explicit modelling of the label as soft.

\subsection{Interpretability: Saliency and Unsupervised Structure}

Two interpretability analyses point in the same direction. Gradient-based saliency on the v4.3 model concentrates at biologically canonical regions --- TIR boundaries for DNA transposons, internal symmetry patterns for LTR elements, and poly(A) tracts for LINEs --- which is reassuring evidence that the multi-scale convolutional encoder learns to detect features that an expert curator would also rely on, rather than spurious genome-specific signatures. The same analysis applied to misclassified sequences shows weak or displaced saliency at canonical positions, consistent with errors arising from features being absent or atypical in those sequences, though attending to non-canonical regions cannot be excluded without additional controls.

Unsupervised contrastive learning (v5) trained on the same encoder recovers the top-level class structure with no class supervision: the UMAP projection separates DNA, LTR and LINE clusters. Within each class, agglomerative hierarchical clustering yields 252 subclusters with modest but non-zero correspondence to annotated superfamilies (ARI~=~0.036, NMI~=~0.136, homogeneity~=~0.247, completeness~=~0.094): individual clusters tend to be superfamily-specific, but each superfamily is fragmented across many clusters. Two practical implications follow. First, the strong unsupervised signal supports the view that mechanistic class identity is largely recoverable from nucleotide composition alone, complementing the $k$-mer logistic-regression result of Section~\ref{ssec:fullset}. Second, the within-class subclusters that do not map cleanly to a single annotated superfamily are candidate targets for curation: they may reflect sequence subvariants, degenerate copies, chimeric assemblies, or genuinely under-annotated subfamilies finer-grained than the current Pantera labels, and unsupervised mining of the embedding space gives a tractable way to surface them for expert review.

\subsection{Limitations and Future Directions}\label{ssec:limitations}

The most concrete limitation of the present work is at the architectural level. All models from v1 to v4.3 share an auxiliary boundary-prediction head that reads from the masked global average pool; in v1 this head is the primary task, while in v3 and v4.x it is a secondary regulariser. In all cases the head is by construction position-invariant and trained against per-epoch random placement on the canvas. As reported in Section~\ref{ssec:tir}, this recovers sequence \emph{length} well (Pearson $r \approx 0.94$) but is essentially uninformative about absolute start and end positions ($r \approx 0.07$--$0.18$). The failure is structural, not statistical: the head literally cannot see where on the canvas the sequence sits. An in-progress v4.4 variant addresses both root causes simultaneously by (i) appending a small bank of sinusoidal positional channels to the one-hot input, so that absolute canvas position becomes available to the convolutional layers, and (ii) replacing the pooled regression head with a per-position $1\!\times\!1$ convolution that produces a sequence-vs-padding logit at every spatial location and is trained with binary cross-entropy against the downsampled canvas mask. The model has been written but not yet retrained at scale; if it improves boundary localisation without harming classification, it is the natural replacement for v4.3 as the final-form encoder.

Beyond v4.4, three broader directions seem worth exploring. The cross-species generalisation gap motivates either explicit phylogenetic stratification of the training set or domain-adaptation methods that learn lineage-agnostic features. The asymmetry between class-level and superfamily-level performance suggests that bringing in protein-level information --- e.g.\ transposase or reverse-transcriptase domain features for the autonomous elements --- could disambiguate superfamilies that share nucleotide composition but differ in their encoded enzymes. Finally, the unsupervised clustering results raise the question of whether DNA-sequence foundation models, pre-trained at scale and then fine-tuned on Pantera, would provide a stronger encoder than the bespoke tower CNN used here; preliminary attempts in this direction were not pursued in the present project but would be a natural next step.
