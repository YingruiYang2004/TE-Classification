\section*{Summary}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Summary}

Transposable elements (TEs) are mobile DNA sequences that occupy a substantial fraction of every vertebrate genome. Their accurate annotation underpins comparative genomics and the study of genome evolution, but existing tools rely on alignment to curated reference libraries and on manual curation, which scale poorly with the volume of data now produced by long-read assembly projects and degrade on divergent or lineage-specific families. This project develops deep-learning classifiers trained directly on the Pantera catalogue of approximately 145{,}000 TE consensi from the Vertebrate Genomes Project (VGP), and asks how far convolutional and graph-based representations of raw DNA sequence can replace alignment as the basis for TE classification.

Four models are presented in sequence. A reverse-complement-invariant tower CNN with multi-scale and dilated convolutions is first trained for terminal-inverted-repeat (TIR) detection, where careful subsampling outperforms loss-based reweightings. A hierarchical CNN classifier (v3) extends this encoder with joint binary (DNA vs non-DNA) and seven-way DNA-superfamily heads, reaching 91.3\% binary accuracy and 94.4\% superfamily accuracy on a held-out test set. A hybrid CNN+GNN model (v4) adds a parallel windowed canonical-7-mer graph branch fused to the CNN through a learned attention gate; the gate exposes a task-dependent split between local motifs and global composition. Broadening the head to three top-level classes and 23 superfamilies (v4.3), with three species withheld for external benchmarking, gives 98.2\% three-class accuracy (macro $F_1 = 0.981$) and 89.3\% superfamily accuracy ($F_1 = 0.839$).

External evaluation on the three held-out species (zebra finch, platypus, American alligator) shows lineage-dependent domain shift: top-level transfer is reasonable on platypus (73.8\%) and alligator (60.1\%) but collapses on zebra finch (33.3\%), where both LTR (F1~=~0.21) and DNA (F1~=~0.35) class discrimination are substantially degraded. Gradient-based saliency analysis confirms that the model concentrates evidence at biologically canonical regions (TIR boundaries for DNA elements, poly(A) tracts for LINEs), and semi-supervised contrastive learning on the same encoder recovers the top-level class structure and reveals candidate subclusters within DNA superfamilies that warrant curation.

Two cross-cutting findings emerge. First, top-level class discrimination is intrinsically easier and more robust than fine-grained superfamily assignment: superfamily heads overfit substantially more than class heads, transfer worse across lineages, and benefit much less from the GNN branch. Second, the boundary-prediction failure observed throughout v1--v4.3 (length is recovered at $r \approx 0.94$ but absolute canvas position is not, $r \approx 0.07$--$0.18$) is structural rather than statistical: the regression head reads from a position-invariant pooled embedding. An architectural fix --- sinusoidal positional channels and a per-position segmentation head --- is sketched as the natural next iteration of the model.

\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Transposable Elements}

% AUDIT(L,C) [L1,C4]: narrowed vertebrate-fraction claim to human; added
% qualifier for other vertebrates.
Transposable elements (TEs) are mobile DNA sequences that propagate copies of themselves within their host genome \cite{mcclintock1950,wells2020field}. Eukaryotic genomes are extensively colonised by TEs: they constitute roughly half of the human genome, with fractions ranging from under 10\% to over 80\% across other vertebrates \cite{wells2020field}, and contribute to chromosomal architecture, gene regulation and species-specific adaptation. Some TE families are autonomous, encoding the enzymes (transposase or reverse transcriptase) required for their own mobilisation; others are non-autonomous, parasitising the machinery of an active autonomous driver \cite{wicker2007unified,wells2020field}.

Knowing which TE families are present in a newly assembled genome, and which copies belong to which family, is a prerequisite for almost every downstream genomic analysis: variant interpretation, comparative genomics, and the study of TE--host coevolution all rely on accurate TE annotation. Existing tools rely on alignment to curated reference libraries together with hand-tuned feature detection and manual curation \cite{smit2015repeatmasker,flynn2020repeatmodeler2}. These approaches scale poorly with the volume of data now produced by long-read assembly projects, and they perform especially badly on divergent or novel families and on non-autonomous elements that are difficult to assign to a driver. A learned classifier that can be trained directly from large catalogues of unannotated insertions would therefore be a valuable complement to alignment-based pipelines.

\subsection{Sequence Features of TE Classes}

The classification task is structured by the biology of how each TE class moves \cite{wicker2007unified,wells2020field}. DNA transposons (Class~II) move by a cut-and-paste mechanism, with autonomous members encoding a transposase that recognises terminal inverted repeats (TIRs) at the boundaries of the element. TIRs are typically 10--50~bp \cite{wicker2007unified}, near-perfect reverse complements of one another, forming a structural signature that a sequence classifier can exploit locally. Class~I elements move via an RNA intermediate. LTR retrotransposons are flanked by direct long terminal repeats (typically 200--600~bp) \cite{wicker2007unified} carrying the regulatory elements for transcription, and internally encode a polyprotein with reverse transcriptase and integrase domains. Long interspersed nuclear elements (LINEs) lack LTRs and instead end with a poly(A) tail; they replicate by target-primed reverse transcription and the autonomous members encode an endonuclease together with a reverse transcriptase.

Two structural features of TE sequences directly constrain the architecture of any classifier. First, a TE may be sequenced from either strand, so a classifier should produce the same prediction for a sequence and its reverse complement (RC). This RC symmetry is a hard constraint that is most cleanly imposed inside the model rather than via test-time augmentation. Second, a TIR is by definition a reverse-complement match between the two ends of the element \cite{wicker2007unified}; detecting it requires comparing positions that may be several kilobases apart in a sequence whose total length varies by two orders of magnitude across families.

\subsection{Previous Work and the Pantera Catalogue}

The most widely used tool for TE annotation, RepeatMasker \cite{smit2015repeatmasker}, identifies repeats by aligning a query genome against a curated library such as Dfam \cite{storer2021dfam} or Repbase. Its output is therefore only as good as the underlying library: divergent or lineage-specific families are missed or systematically misclassified. Several recent efforts have applied machine learning to TE detection or sub-classification\,---\,including deep-learning classifiers such as DeepTE \cite{yan2020deepte} and TERL \cite{requena2021terl}; however, most have either focused on a single TE class or been trained on the same curated reference libraries they are meant to extend, limiting their ability to generalise to novel or lineage-specific families.

The starting point for this project is the Pantera catalogue, which uses pangenome polymorphisms to detect TE insertions that are absent in some haplotypes of a species \cite{sierra2024pantera}. Applied to the Vertebrate Genomes Project (VGP) assemblies \cite{rhie2021vgp}, Pantera produces a catalogue of approximately 145{,}000 sequences from over fifty vertebrate species, with FASTA-header annotations giving the broad TE class (DNA, LTR, LINE, SINE, RC, \dots) and, where assignable, the superfamily. Because Pantera detects elements directly from polymorphism, it provides an unusually large, alignment-independent training set on which a learned classifier can be built without circularly inheriting the biases of a reference library. The dataset is, however, heavily skewed: roughly 92\% of sequences carry no transposase annotation, DNA transposons constitute only about 8\%, and many superfamilies have fewer than a hundred examples.

\subsection{Convolutional Neural Networks for Sequence Classification}

Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) \cite{lecun2015deep,krizhevsky2012imagenet,alipanahi2015deepbind} are a natural choice for detecting short, position-invariant motifs in biological sequences. A 1-D convolution over a one-hot-encoded sequence learns a bank of filters whose activations indicate where in the sequence each motif occurs; stacking convolutions with non-linearities then composes these motifs into longer patterns. Two architectural ingredients matter for TE sequences in particular. \emph{Multi-scale} convolutions, with several kernel widths in parallel, allow the network to capture both short, locally conserved motifs and longer structural features in the same forward pass. \emph{Dilated} convolutions enlarge the receptive field exponentially with depth without an equivalent growth in parameters \cite{yu2016dilated}, which is necessary to span TIRs whose two halves may be several kilobases apart.

CNNs also have well-known limitations. After global pooling, all positional information is averaged out, so a CNN-only classifier cannot directly use absolute or relative position within the sequence. Within the receptive field of a given filter, the precise spatial layout of nucleotides is preserved, but anything more global must be re-learned from indirect cues such as motif density.

\subsection{Graph Neural Networks for Compositional Information}\label{ssec:gnn_intro}

A complementary view of a TE sequence is its $k$-mer composition: the multiset of all length-$k$ substrings, which captures global compositional bias and many long-range correlations that motif filters do not see. Even a logistic regression on canonical 7-mer frequencies separates the three top-level classes (DNA, LTR, LINE) at roughly 85\% macro $F_1$ (Section~\ref{ssec:fullset}; Figure~\ref{fig:kmer_separation}), establishing that compositional signal alone is highly informative for class-level identification. However, a single global $k$-mer histogram discards positional structure entirely, and a fixed positional binning would tie the representation to a particular sequence length.

A graph neural network (GNN) \cite{kipf2016semi,hamilton2017inductive,zhou2019graph} provides a flexible compromise. The sequence is broken into overlapping windows; each window contributes a node whose feature vector is its windowed $k$-mer profile, and adjacent windows are connected by edges. Message passing then mixes information between neighbouring windows over several layers, producing a global representation that retains coarse positional structure (which compositional pattern appears where along the sequence) without requiring the input to be padded to a fixed length. Using \emph{canonical} $k$-mers (the lexicographically smaller of a $k$-mer and its reverse complement) keeps the representation strand-symmetric, in line with the RC invariance discussed above.

\subsection{Attention Fusion and Hierarchical Heads}

CNN and GNN branches see complementary aspects of the same sequence: the CNN sees local motifs, the GNN sees windowed composition. There is no \emph{a priori} reason to prefer a fixed weighting of the two; some sequences may be classifiable from a single sharp motif and others from broad compositional shifts. An attention-style gating layer \cite{vaswani2017attention} learns a per-input convex combination of the two branch embeddings, so that the fused representation can lean on whichever modality is locally more informative.

The downstream classifier is itself hierarchical. A binary head distinguishes DNA transposons from all other TEs (or, in later versions, separates the three top-level classes DNA, LTR and LINE), while a superfamily head provides the finer assignment within each top-level class. This mirrors both the biological hierarchy of TE classification and the empirical observation, returned to throughout the Results, that top-level discrimination is intrinsically easier and more robust than fine-grained superfamily assignment.

This thesis develops these ideas through a sequence of progressively more capable models. After describing the data and architectures (Section~\ref{sec:methods}), I evaluate a TIR-detection CNN as a proof of concept, a hierarchical CNN classifier (v3) for DNA superfamilies, a hybrid CNN+GNN model (v4) that adds compositional signal via a $k$-mer graph, and a three-class extension (v4.3) that is also evaluated on three held-out species as an external benchmark (Section~\ref{sec:results}). Two interpretability analyses --- gradient-based saliency and semi-supervised contrastive clustering --- close the Results before the Discussion.

\section{Data and Methods}\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Data}\label{ssec:data}

The training data are transposable element sequences identified by Pantera \cite{sierra2024pantera} from the Vertebrate Genomes Project (VGP) assemblies. Pantera detects TE families directly from pangenome polymorphisms by identifying insertions present in some haplotypes and absent in others, and therefore produces a comprehensive catalogue of TEs without inheriting the family list of any particular reference library. The catalogue used here contains approximately 145{,}000 sequences from over fifty vertebrate species. 

In particular, the dataset includes 11{,}500 TIR-positive sequences that are used to train the v1 TIR-detection model. The full set of sequences, with class and superfamily labels parsed from the FASTA headers, is used for the v3 and v4 models. The v4.3 three-class model and the external benchmark (Section~\ref{ssec:mini_benchmark}) use a subset of the full set with only DNA, LTR and LINE classes.

The class distribution is heavily imbalanced: unannotated sequences (``None'') account for 62.3\% of the dataset, with LTR (18.8\%), LINE (10.4\%) and DNA transposons (7.9\%) making up the major part of the labelled fraction. Among the labelled classes, DNA transposons are the focus of the fine-grained superfamily classifier described in Sections~\ref{ssec:methods_v3} and~\ref{ssec:methods_v4}, and the imbalance within DNA is particularly severe: only seven superfamilies (DNA/hAT, DNA/TcMar-Tc1, DNA/PIF-Harbinger, DNA/PiggyBac, DNA/Academ-1, DNA/CMC, DNA/Maverick) exceed 100 examples, which is a practical minimum needed to train a reliable classification head. Even among these, counts span more than an order of magnitude, from 4{,}900 (DNA/hAT) to 129 (DNA/Maverick). Sequence lengths vary considerably (173~bp to 19{,}907~bp; median 3{,}263~bp), reflecting the biological diversity of TE types. For the v4.3 three-class model and the external benchmark (Section~\ref{ssec:mini_benchmark}), three species are excluded from \emph{both} training and validation and reserved as a held-out external test set: Zebra finch (\textit{Taeniopygia guttata}), platypus (\textit{Ornithorhynchus anatinus}), and American alligator (\textit{Alligator mississippiensis}). For each of these species, a mini-benchmark dataset was assembled comprising three complementary sources: (i)~Pantera-derived consensus sequences identified in that species' genome, (ii)~an externally curated RepeatMasker-format TE reference library with \texttt{Name\#Class/Subclass} annotations, and (iii)~a set of structurally detected LTR retrotransposons produced by genome-wide alignment of LTR terminal repeats. The evaluation in Section~\ref{ssec:mini_benchmark} uses the curated libraries as the primary ground-truth set; the Pantera consensi and structural LTR sequences are additional reference data accompanying the benchmark.

\subsection{Sequence Representation}\label{ssec:repr}

All models take one-hot encoded DNA sequences as input. Each nucleotide is mapped to one of five channels (A, C, G, T, N), where N covers ambiguous or non-standard bases. Channels are stacked along the feature dimension to produce a tensor of shape (5, $L$). 

Because CNNs require a fixed input length, every sequence is placed on a fixed-length canvas ( 20~kb by default). In each epoch, for a sequence of length $\ell$, a start position $s$ is sampled uniformly from $\{0, 1, \dots, L-\ell\}$, the sequence occupies positions $s$ through $s+\ell-1$, and the rest of the canvas is filled with zero columns. A binary mask records which positions carry real sequence; the mask is consumed by the masked global pooling layer so that padded positions do not contribute to the pooled embedding. Random placement during training also serves as positional augmentation: the model sees each sequence at many different absolute offsets across epochs.

Reverse-complement invariance is built into the encoding. The reverse complement of a one-hot tensor is obtained by reversing the spatial axis and remapping channels (A$\leftrightarrow$T, C$\leftrightarrow$G); this is a deterministic operation that adds no new parameters. Both the forward and the reverse-complement views of a sequence are passed through the encoder during the forward pass and fused for later embeddings (Section~\ref{ssec:methods_v3}).

\subsection{Model v1: TIR Detection CNN}\label{ssec:methods_v1}

The v1 model is a simple, single-task binary classifier for TIR presence. The encoder is a multi-scale tower CNN: the input one-hot tensor is processed through three parallel convolutional branches with kernel widths 7, 15 and 21~bp, each producing 128 feature maps. The three branches are concatenated along the channel dimension and mixed by a $1\!\times\!1$ convolution back to 128 channels. Four dilated convolutional blocks with kernel size 9 and dilation rates $\{1, 2, 4, 8\}$ then expand the receptive field, each block using GELU activation, batch normalisation, dropout (0.15) and a residual connection. A masked global average pool produces a 128-dimensional sequence embedding, which is fed to a small binary classification head.

In particular, the model needs to response identically to a sequence and its reverse complement, and this is achieved by late fusion: the same encoder is run on the forward sequence and on the rc-sequence, and the two resulting embeddings are averaged before the classification head. An earlier variant fused immediately after the first convolutional layer. However, late fusion is both architecturally simpler (no need for RC-symmetric convolutional weights) and at least as accurate, for the reasons discussed in Section~\ref{ssec:hybrid_disc}.

\subsection{Model v3: Hierarchical CNN Classifier}\label{ssec:methods_v3}

The v3 classifier uses the same tower CNN encoder as v1, but the pooled embedding is feeded to two parallel heads: a binary head that distinguishes DNA transposons from all other TE classes, and a seven-way superfamily head classifying over the DNA superfamilies with at least 100 training examples. Both heads are trained jointly, with the superfamily loss masked out for non-DNA samples so that only DNA examples contribute to the superfamily gradient. This hierarchical design reflects the biological organisation of the labels: DNA transposons share structural features that separate them from retrotransposons, and within DNA, finer motifs distinguish superfamilies.

The class imbalance described in Section~\ref{ssec:data} is addressed at two levels. The non-DNA majority class is subsampled from approximately 135{,}000 to 20{,}000 sequences before training, which keeps enough negatives to learn a reliable binary boundary while preventing the model from collapsing to a constant prediction. Within DNA, superfamilies with fewer than 100 examples are excluded from the multi-class head, leaving the seven superfamilies above. The remaining superfamily imbalance is handled by inverse square-root class weighting in the superfamily loss. After this filtering and balancing, the v3 training set contains 20{,}000 non-DNA sequences and 30{,}431 DNA sequences across seven superfamilies, with 6{,}141 sequences held out for testing.

\subsection{Model v4: Hybrid CNN + k-mer GNN}\label{ssec:methods_v4}

Based on the intuition discussion in Section~\ref{ssec:gnn_intro}, the v4 hybrid model adds a parallel $k$-mer graph neural network branch to the v3 encoder. The CNN branch is unchanged; the GNN branch operates on a sequence-level graph constructed as follows. The sequence is divided into 512~bp sliding windows with stride 256~bp (50\% overlap). For each window, all $k=7$ subsequences are extracted and counted using \emph{canonical} $k$-mers (the lexicographically smaller of the $k$-mer and its reverse complement) so that the representation is strand-symmetric, then hashed into a fixed-size feature vector of dimension 2048. A short relative positional encoding is concatenated to each window's feature vector to keep coarse positional information. The windows form the nodes of a chain graph in which adjacent windows are connected by bidirectional edges with self-loops. A 3-layer GraphSAGE-style \cite{hamilton2017inductive} message-passing GNN with hidden dimension 128 then aggregates information across neighbouring windows, and a scatter mean pool produces a single 128-dimensional graph-level embedding.

The CNN and GNN embeddings are combined by an attention-style gate. A small MLP takes the concatenation of the two embeddings as input and outputs a two-element softmax, and the fused embedding is the convex combination of the softmax. The fused embedding is then fed to the same hierarchical heads as in v3. 

The same architecture is used for the three-class extensions v4.2 and v4.3, with only the top-level head broadened from binary to three-way (DNA / LTR / LINE) and the superfamily head broadened from 7 to 23 outputs. v4.2 is trained on the full VGP set; v4.3 additionally excludes the three benchmark species (Section~\ref{ssec:data}) and caps training at 40 epochs to mitigate the overfitting observed in v4.2 (Section~\ref{ssec:fullset}).

\subsection{Model v5: Contrastive Embedding}\label{ssec:methods_v5}

The v5 model repurposes the pretrained v4 hybrid encoder (CNN tower + $k$-mer GNN, weights frozen at the start of fine-tuning) for semi-supervised clustering of DNA transposons. The classification head is replaced by a 128-dimensional L2-normalised projection head, and the encoder is fine-tuned with a combined multi-task loss. Initial experiments used supervised contrastive loss (SupCon) alone, which pulls embeddings from sequences of the same superfamily together while pushing apart those from different superfamilies. While this encourages well-clustered embeddings, pure SupCon can push sequences apart in ways that are not biologically meaningful, particularly when negative pairs are sampled from superfamilies with broadly similar sequence composition; early runs required hard-coded loss clipping to prevent excessive separation, which is not a principled solution. The final implementation addresses this by combining three loss terms:
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{SupCon}} + \lambda_{\text{cls}} \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}} + \lambda_{\text{ctr}} \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{center}}
\]
where $\mathcal{L}_{\text{SupCon}}$ is the supervised contrastive loss \cite{khosla2020supervised} with temperature $\tau = 0.3$, which pulls same-superfamily embeddings together and pushes different-superfamily embeddings apart; $\mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}}$ is a cross-entropy classification loss over the DNA superfamilies, which anchors the representation to the known label structure and prevents embedding collapse; and $\mathcal{L}_{\text{center}}$ is a center loss that explicitly pulls embeddings toward learned per-class centroids to further tighten within-class clusters. The weights are $\lambda_{\text{cls}} = 0.5$ and $\lambda_{\text{ctr}} = 0.01$. After training, the 256-dimensional fused embeddings (before the projection head) are extracted, projected to 2-D with UMAP, and clustered with HDBSCAN to discover candidate subclusters within each superfamily (Section~\ref{ssec:clustering}).

\subsection{Training}\label{ssec:training}

All models are trained with the AdamW optimiser \cite{loshchilov2019decoupled} ($\mathrm{lr} = 10^{-3}$, weight decay $10^{-4}$) and a cosine annealing schedule. Class imbalance is addressed at two levels, as described above: subsampling for the binary task, and inverse square-root class weighting for the superfamily task. Multi-class heads use label smoothing ($\alpha = 0.1$).

The CNN-only models use batch size 32; the hybrid model uses batch size 16 because of the additional memory required for the graph branch. Note that this difference in batch size is a potential confound when comparing v3 and v4 performance directly. Training uses early stopping with patience of 10 epochs based on the validation macro $F_1$. Five-fold cross-validation was used during model development to guide hyperparameter selection; all performance figures reported in the Results are single-split test-set point estimates on the held-out species-stratified split (80\%/20\%); no standard errors are available for the neural model test scores.

An auxiliary boundary regression head is attached to the pooled embedding during training. It is a two-layer MLP (128$\to$64$\to$3, GELU, sigmoid) that predicts three scalar values: the normalised start position $s/L$, end position $e/L$, and length $\ell/L$ of the real sequence on the canvas; and it is trained with MSE loss weighted at 0.1 of the total loss. The head acts as a regulariser: by forcing the encoder to retain enough spatial information to recover the placement of the sequence, it aims to discourage the model from ignoring the global structure of the input. As discussed in Section~\ref{ssec:tir} and Section~\ref{ssec:limitations}, length predictions are accurate (the length is recoverable from the mask regardless of placement) but absolute start and end positions are not, because the masked average pool is by construction position-invariant. 

\subsection{Evaluation}\label{ssec:evaluation}

Model performance is reported as accuracy, balanced accuracy, macro and weighted $F_1$, and per-class $F_1$. AUROC and AUPRC are reported for binary tasks. For hierarchical models,  top-level (DNA vs non-DNA, or three-class) and superfamily-level metrics are reported seperately, and confusion matrices at both levels are generated. Per-superfamily $F_1$ is reported only for superfamilies with at least 30 test samples to avoid spurious significance from very rare classes. 

For the external benchmark (Section~\ref{ssec:mini_benchmark}), evaluation is performed directly on the curated TE libraries of the three held-out species. Each FASTA header is parsed for top-level class and superfamily, and labels are mapped to the model's vocabulary using a hierarchical strategy: exact superfamily match where available, parent-class fallback (e.g.\ DNA/X $\to$ DNA) when the specific subfamily is unseen, and exclusion from superfamily scoring otherwise. Sequences whose top-level class is outside the model's three-way head (e.g.\ tRNA, SINE, RC/Helitron, Unknown) are excluded from the evaluable set.

All models were implemented in PyTorch 2.0+ and trained either locally on Apple-silicon MPS or on NVIDIA A100 GPU on CSD3 (Cambridge Service for Data Driven Discovery). Training and inference use full-precision FP32; validation losses, validation macro $F_1$ and the learned attention gate weights are logged each epoch and used to drive early stopping. \section{Results}\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Terminal Inverted Repeat Detection}

Terminal inverted repeats (TIRs) are a defining structural feature of DNA transposons, consisting of near-identical sequences in opposite orientations at the element boundaries. Detecting TIR presence is a fundamental task for TE classification, as it distinguishes DNA transposons from other TE classes such as LTR retrotransposons and LINEs. As an introduction to the later deep learning tasks, I started with developing a convolutional neural network (CNN) specifically designed for binary TIR detection, addressing two key challenges: the inherent reverse-complement symmetry of TIRs and severe class imbalance in the training data.

\subsubsection{Handling Class Imbalance}

The TIR-detection dataset is moderately imbalanced: of the 145{,}000 Pantera sequences, approximately 11{,}500 carry a TIR annotation, giving a positive rate of roughly 8\% before subsampling; after stratified subsampling to a balanced training split the effective positive fraction is 50\%. I compared inverse frequency class weighting, inverse square-root weighting, focal loss \cite{lin2017focal}, and stratified subsampling. In all cases the loss-based reweightings introduced training instability (oscillating validation $F_1$) while the balanced subset converged more stably; the simpler approach was therefore adopted.

VGP sequences vary in length from 173~bp to 19{,}907~bp (median 3{,}263~bp). They are placed at random positions within the 40~kb canvas during training (Section~\ref{ssec:repr}), and an auxiliary regression head was attached to the pooled embedding to predict the start, end and length of the sequence within the canvas.

Length prediction works well ($r \approx 0.94$), but absolute start and end positions are essentially unpredictable ($r \approx 0.07$--$0.18$). This is not a training failure but a structural one: the regression head reads from the masked global average pool, which is by construction position-invariant, so it has access to global summary statistics (which correlate with length) but no information about \emph{where} on the canvas the sequence sits. Section~\ref{ssec:limitations} returns to this point and outlines an architectural fix.


\subsection{Hierarchical DNA Transposon Classification (v3)}\label{ssec:hier}

The v3 hierarchical CNN classifier (Section~\ref{ssec:methods_v3}) extends the TIR-detection model to the DNA transposon subclassification task. It is trained on 20{,}000 subsampled non-DNA sequences and 30{,}431 DNA-transposon sequences across the seven DNA superfamilies with sufficient sample size, with 6{,}141 sequences held out for testing. 

\subsubsection{Performance}

The hierarchical model achieves strong performance on both tasks. For binary classification (DNA transposon vs other TE classes), the model reaches 91.3\% accuracy with a macro $F_1$ of 0.87. For superfamily classification among DNA transposons, accuracy is 94.4\% with a macro $F_1$ of 0.90.

Performance varies across superfamilies and broadly tracks training support. DNA/hAT achieves the highest $F_1$ of 0.97; DNA/TcMar-Tc1 is similarly strong ($F_1 = 0.96$). DNA/CMC is the most challenging ($F_1 = 0.68$), consistent with its limited training support (39 test sequences) and highly heterogeneous composition. A separate complication is the generic \textit{DNA} label applied to sequences annotated only at class level in the Pantera headers (no superfamily assignment). These 1{,}530 sequences form an inherently heterogeneous group; the model predicts most of them as \textit{DNA} but with substantially lower mean confidence (0.69) than for sequences with a definite superfamily label ($>$0.85), and reassigns a fraction to specific superfamilies, most frequently DNA/CMC. This accounts for DNA/CMC's low precision (0.55): many of the false positives are generic \textit{DNA} sequences that share compositional features with CMC elements rather than true superfamily misclassifications.

\subsection{Hybrid CNN and K-mer Graph Neural Network Model (v4)}\label{ssec:hybrid}

The v4 hybrid model (Section~\ref{ssec:methods_v4}) augments the CNN tower with a parallel windowed $k$-mer GNN branch and fuses the two embeddings through a learned attention gate, aiming to capture positional and compositional information. It is evaluated first in the same binary (DNA vs non-DNA) setting as v3, then extended to a three-class head in v4.2 / v4.3 (Section~\ref{ssec:fullset}). 

In the binary setting, the learned gate weights remain highly variable throughout training and no branch consistently dominates (Figure~\ref{fig:v4_training}, lower-left panel), suggesting that the model does not prefer a fixed CNN-vs-GNN split for the binary task. Gate weight behaviour differs markedly in the three-class extension: see Figure~\ref{fig:gate_weights} and Section~\ref{ssec:hybrid_disc}. 

\subsubsection{Comparison with the CNN-Only Model}

The hybrid model improves on the CNN-only baseline on both the binary detection and superfamily classification tasks (Table~\ref{tab:v3_v4_sf_f1}; Figure~\ref{fig:v4_confusion}). Binary accuracy rises from 91.3\,\% to 93.1\,\% on the balanced test set (macro $F_1$ 0.87\,$\to$\,0.89). For DNA superfamily classification, macro $F_1$ rises from 0.91 to 0.96, with the gain almost entirely concentrated in DNA/CMC ($F_1$ 0.67\,$\to$\,0.92): in v3 the CMC class suffers low precision (0.51) due to confusion with the generic DNA-labelled sequences, whereas the GNN's $k$-mer composition signal provides the additional discriminant that resolves this ambiguity. The five remaining specific superfamilies change by at most $\pm0.01$ $F_1$, confirming that local sequence motifs captured by the CNN remain the primary discriminant for those fine-grained distinctions. A standalone $k$-mer GNN trained on 33 TE classes achieves AUROC\,=\,0.905 for binary DNA detection but only 41\,\% balanced accuracy across individual classes, further demonstrating that global $k$-mer composition encodes class-level but not superfamily-level discriminative information (Section~\ref{ssec:fullset}; Figure~\ref{fig:kmer_separation} and Table~\ref{tab:kmer_within_class}).

However, the picture changes on the full imbalanced dataset. v4 achieves a much lower FNR than v3 (1.5\% vs 8.9\%), nearly eliminating missed DNA sequences, but at the cost of a substantially higher FPR (10.8\% vs 6.3\%). On the full 125{,}098 non-DNA sequences this produces 13{,}480 false positives against 10{,}100 true positives, meaning a precision of only 43\%, worse than v3. The two models thus represent different operating points on the recall--precision curve: v3 is more conservative (fewer false alarms, more missed DNA); v4 is more sensitive (almost no missed DNA, but substantially more contamination of the superfamily classifier).

\subsection{Multi-Class Extension: Three-Class Classification (v4.2 and v4.3)}\label{ssec:fullset}

The binary models described in Sections~\ref{ssec:hier} and~\ref{ssec:hybrid} classify DNA transposons against all other TEs, which are being placed in a single non-DNA category. This formulation discards the finer-grained supervision available in the dataset. In this section, the hybrid CNN+GNN model is extended to a three-class head distinguishing DNA transposons, LTR retrotransposons, and LINEs, developing two successive versions: v4.2, trained on the full VGP dataset, and v4.3, which reserves three benchmark species for external validation.

\subsubsection{Three-Class Reformulation}

The three-class head asks the model to distinguish: cut-and-paste DNA transposons; LTR retrotransposons, which copy via an RNA intermediate flanked by long terminal repeats; and LINEs, which replicate through target-primed reverse transcription. Sequences with ambiguous or rare class labels (SINE, PLE, RC entries) are excluded from training and evaluation due to severe class imbalance as they only contribute to less than 1\% of the whole dataset (SINE: 0.42\%, PLE: 0.20\%, RC: 0.12\%); unannotated sequences are also excluded due to potential uncontrollable diversity among these sequences. After filtering, 50,431 sequences remain distributed across 23 superfamilies. To further balance representation, each superfamily's training contribution is capped at 3,000 sequences, so the effective training set contains 30,727 sequences. The CNN and GNN tower architectures are unchanged from the hybrid model (Section~\ref{ssec:hybrid}); only the top-level classification head is broadened from two outputs to three, with superfamily subheads spanning all three major classes.

\subsubsection{Overfitting Analysis in v4.2}

The first three-class version (v4.2) was trained on the full VGP dataset for up to 65 epochs. However, the result is concerning: both training and validation superfamily F1 reaching 0.999, while the held-out test set's superfamily macro F1 dropped to 0.872. The gap of 0.127 shows a clear signal of moderate overfitting (Table~\ref{tab:v4_2_splits}). On the other hand, the F1 score for the top-level three-class head generalized much better (test F1 = 0.984, gap = 0.016), indicating that three-class discrimination is inherently robust while fine-grained superfamily classification is susceptible to memorizing genome-specific sequence patterns. 

This overfitting pattern emerged despite inverse square-root class weighting and early stopping on validation macro $F_1$, consistent with a scenario in which the model fits genome-specific subvariants of rare superfamilies during training. This may be induced by the class imbalance and the presence of rare superfamilies. Especially for superfamilies with fewer than 500 training samples, the model can memorize genome-specific subvariants rather than learning generalizable features. Analysis of prediction confidence revealed that on test samples the model maintained high confidence even for misclassifications, indicating overconfident decision boundaries rather than principled uncertainty. 

To diagnose this, I examined the 65-epoch training trajectory (Figure~\ref{fig:v4_2_training}): training loss decreases steadily to a plateau around $\approx$0.44, while validation class and superfamily F1 scores climb monotonically throughout, reaching near-perfect values (class F1~$>$~0.99, SF F1~$\approx$~1.00) by epoch 65. Such sustained improvement in validation metrics with only minor gains in training loss is the signature of the model fitting the validation set as a result of the checkpoint selection criterion interacting with a small and compositionally similar validation split. The attention gate converges with the CNN branch dominating ($\approx$0.88) and the GNN branch suppressed ($\approx$0.12) throughout training.

\subsubsection{Benchmark Exclusion and Final Model (v4.3)}

The final model (v4.3) excludes three species from both training and evaluation: Zebra finch (\textit{Taeniopygia guttata}), platypus (\textit{Ornithorhynchus anatinus}), and American alligator (\textit{Alligator mississippiensis}). These species are reserved for an independent external benchmark (Section~\ref{ssec:mini_benchmark}), ensuring that no TEs from these genomes influence learned sequence features. Training was capped at 40 epochs with early stopping on macro F1, and the resulting held-out test set spans 6,141 sequences from the remaining VGP genomes.

\subsubsection{Performance (v4.3)}

The v4.3 model achieves 98.2\% accuracy with a macro F1 of 0.981 on the top-level three-class task (DNA vs LTR vs LINE). All three classes are well-separated: DNA (F1~=~0.978), LTR (F1~=~0.983), and LINE (F1~=~0.983), with per-class accuracy of 97.3\%, 98.2\%, and 98.9\% respectively.

At the superfamily level, accuracy is 89.3\% and macro F1 is 0.839 across 23 superfamilies. The most frequent misclassification patterns are biologically interpretable: DNA/hAT sequences are confused with the generic DNA label and vice versa ($n$~=~33 and 24); LTR/Gypsy and LTR/Pao are mutually confused ($n$~=~27 and 18); and ERV elements are confused with the unspecified LTR label. All 15 most common misclassifications occur within the same top-level class (DNA$\rightarrow$DNA, LTR$\rightarrow$LTR, LINE$\rightarrow$LINE), confirming that the model reliably identifies the transposition mechanism even when the precise superfamily is ambiguous.

This strong baseline separability confirms the motivation for the GNN component of the hybrid architecture stated in Section~\ref{ssec:gnn_intro}: compositional signals provide strong priors for top-level class identity, and the attention gate can lean on this modality when local motifs are ambiguous.

\subsection{External Benchmark: Mini Dataset Evaluation}\label{ssec:mini_benchmark}

A held-out test set drawn from the same VGP distribution provides evidence of generalisation within the training distribution. A more stringent assessment requires species never encountered in any stage of training. The three species withheld from v4.3, Zebra finch (\textit{T.~guttata}), platypus (\textit{O.~anatinus}), and American alligator (\textit{A.~mississippiensis}), form a mini benchmark spanning the three main amniote lineages (Aves, Mammalia, Reptilia), each with a distinct TE landscape (Table~\ref{tab:mini_benchmark_species}).

\subsubsection{Benchmark Species}

The mini-benchmark dataset for each species comprises three complementary sources. First, Pantera consensus sequences detected in each genome using the same pipeline as the main training set. Second, an externally curated RepeatMasker-format TE reference library with \texttt{Name\#Class/Subclass} annotations, which serves as the primary evaluation set. Third, a set of structurally detected LTR retrotransposons produced by genome-wide pairwise alignment of LTR terminal repeats: each sequence carries full genomic coordinate metadata (scaffold, flanking regions, LTR arm identity, and target-site duplication), and all are confirmed as LTR class, though no superfamily annotation is provided.

Birds typically have compact genomes dominated by CR1 LINEs with relatively few DNA transposons; the Zebra finch ($\sim$1.0 Gb genome) is a well-studied avian model with an established curated TE library of 543 entries and 6{,}480 structurally detected LTR sequences. Platypus is an early-branching monotreme mammal with an unusually rich and active TE repertoire, including families rare in therian mammals; its curated library contains 380 entries and 49{,}814 structural LTR sequences. American alligator represents Crocodylia with a moderately sized genome ($\sim$2.2 Gb) and a TE composition intermediate between birds and mammals, with 1{,}196 curated reference entries and 15{,}557 structural LTR sequences providing dense ground-truth coverage.

Sequences with labels outside the model scope (e.g., tRNA, SINE, RC/Helitron, Unknown) are excluded from the evaluable set.

\subsection{Saliency-Based Attribution Analysis}\label{ssec:saliency}

To understand which sequence regions drive model predictions, input-level gradients (saliency maps) \cite{simonyan2014deep} is computed and analysised using backpropagation of the logit vector through the network to the input. For each test sequence, the gredient $\nabla_x L$ is computed where $L$ is the cross-entropy loss for the true class. The resulting raw gradients are $\ell_2$-normalised per sequence so that scores have unit norm, with positive values indicating positions that increase the true-class logit and negative values indicating positions that suppress it.

\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{saliency_analysis.png}
\caption{Occlusion-based saliency for four representative misclassified test sequences chosen from the dominant superfamily confusion pairs identified in Figure~\ref{fig:test_confusion_structure} (DNA/hAT$\rightarrow$DNA, LTR/Gypsy$\rightarrow$LTR/Pao, DNA$\rightarrow$DNA/hAT, LINE/L1$\rightarrow$LINE/L1-Tx1). For each example, two curves show the per-position drop in the true-class logit (blue) and in the predicted-class logit (orange) when a sliding window is masked, so positive values mark regions important for the corresponding class. The panels reveals that a confident error like LINE/L1$\rightarrow$LINE/L1-Tx1 typically shows broadly negative true-class saliency across the body, while ambiguous within-class errors (LTR/Gypsy$\leftrightarrow$LTR/Pao) show overlapping true- and predicted-class envelopes consistent with shared internal motifs.}
\label{fig:saliency_analysis}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:saliency_analysis} examines four representative misclassified sequences drawn from the dominant superfamily confusion pairs. Rather than aggregated per-superfamily summaries, the panels illustrate the local logit-attribution structure of individual errors. For the DNA/hAT$\rightarrow$DNA case, the true-class saliency localises to a few sharp regions while the predicted (DNA-generic) curve is comparatively diffuse, consistent with the model finding the canonical hAT signal weak and falling back on a broader DNA-class feature. For the within-class LTR/Gypsy$\rightarrow$LTR/Pao confusion, the two curves track a shared internal envelope, indicating the sequence carries motifs compatible with both superfamilies. For the LINE/L1$\rightarrow$LINE/L1-Tx1 case, true-class saliency is broadly negative across the sequence, consistent with a confident subfamily flip rather than a localised feature failure. 

A per-superfamily comparison of saliency stereotypy, for example whether DNA/hAT's higher F1 (0.896) compared to DNA/CMC (0.494) is associated with sharper or more spatially concentrated attribution profiles, was not carried out in this work. The observed F1 variance is therefore consistent with, but not directly evidence for, a feature-stereotypy explanation.

\subsection{Semi-supervised Clustering of TE Sequences}\label{ssec:clustering}

In addition to supervised classification, I explored semi-supervised contrastive learning to test whether the model's learned embeddings capture biologically meaningful structure beyond what the supervised head requires. The v5 model architecture and combined loss are described in Section~\ref{ssec:methods_v5}; this section analyses the resulting embedding geometry and clustering structure.

To identify structure within classes, I applied two complementary clustering analyses to the embedding space. \section{Discussion}

\subsection{When Does the GNN Branch Help?}\label{ssec:hybrid_disc}

The hybrid CNN+GNN architecture was motivated by the hypothesis that local motif filters and global compositional features see complementary aspects of a TE sequence and that combining them via a learned attention gate would outperform either branch alone. The results bear this out only in part. The GNN contributes a clear improvement on the broad class-level task (DNA vs non-DNA in v4, three-way DNA/LTR/LINE in v4.3). The learned gate behaves differently across the two settings: in the binary v4 model the gate weights remain highly variable throughout training with no branch consistently dominant over 40 epochs; in the three-class v4.3 model the GNN weight converges to $\sim$0.72 (CNN $\sim$0.28) from approximately epoch 25 onward (Figure~\ref{fig:gate_weights}). Superfamily classification within DNA is essentially indistinguishable between the hybrid and the pure CNN of v3. This pattern is consistent with the supporting analysis in Section~\ref{ssec:fullset}: a logistic regression on canonical $k$-mer frequencies already recovers most of the top-level class structure, while within-class superfamily separation is poor. The mechanistic features that distinguish DNA transposons, LTR retrotransposons and LINEs --- and which are mirrored in their nucleotide-composition statistics --- are exactly what the GNN sees most clearly; the finer distinctions between superfamilies in the same class depend on local motifs that only the CNN can resolve. The attention gate is therefore best read as a soft prior on which modality to trust for a given input, not as a generic improvement on top of the CNN.

A second, more procedural lesson concerns reverse-complement invariance. An earlier variant of the encoder enforced RC symmetry by symmetrising the one-hot input and fusing immediately after the first convolutional layer. Late fusion --- running the CNN twice, once per strand, and averaging the resulting embeddings --- proved both simpler and at least as accurate. The likely reason is that early fusion forces the very first filters to be RC-symmetric, which restricts the family of representable motifs; late fusion allows each pass to learn strand-specific intermediate features that are only reconciled at the embedding level, while still guaranteeing exact RC invariance.

\subsection{Generalisation and Domain Shift}

The contrast between the within-VGP test set (v4.3 three-class accuracy 98.2\%) and the three held-out amniote species (overall 55.0\%, zebra finch 33.3\%) is the strongest evidence in this work that TE sequence features show substantial lineage-dependence, at least across the amniote clades sampled here. Within-class breakdowns localise the failure: LTR discrimination is consistently the weakest class across all three species (F1~=~0.21--0.50), with avian-specific ERV families the main contributors to error. DNA-class performance is partially preserved in platypus (F1~=~0.81) and alligator (F1~=~0.70) but also collapses in zebra finch (F1~=~0.35), indicating that avian DNA transposons carry features not represented in the mammalian and reptilian training set. Mammalian and reptilian sequences in the training set therefore lack the avian-specific signatures for both LTR and DNA classes, and a model trained without avian examples has no way to recover either.

This is a constraint on the training distribution rather than a deficiency of the architecture: the same encoder that fails on zebra finch LTR transfers reasonably well to platypus, whose TE landscape is closer to the mammalian and reptilian sequences seen during training. Two complementary remedies are natural. The first is simply broader phylogenetic coverage in the training set, with explicit stratification across vertebrate lineages so that each major branch contributes LTR examples. The second is architectural: domain-adversarial training~\cite{ganin2015unsupervised} and model-agnostic meta-learning~\cite{finn2017model} have been applied in other biological domains to learn representations that transfer across distribution shifts; analogous approaches could encourage the encoder to learn lineage-agnostic TE features, at the cost of additional training-time complexity.

\subsection{Overfitting Patterns Across the Hierarchy}

A consistent observation across v3, v4 and especially v4.2 is that superfamily heads overfit substantially more than top-level class heads on the same training run. In v4.2 the superfamily test gap reached 0.127 while the three-class gap was only 0.016. The asymmetry is again consistent with the broader picture: top-level classes are large, well-supported and biologically distinct, so their decision boundary is constrained by many examples; superfamilies are smaller, internally heterogeneous and partly defined by alignment to reference libraries, so the model has both the capacity and the incentive to memorise genome-specific subvariants.

The mitigations adopted in v4.3 --- early stopping at 40 epochs (just before the validation-loss inflection observed in v4.2), label smoothing, and explicit species hold-out --- reduced but did not eliminate the gap, and the residual gap is concentrated in the rarest superfamilies (e.g.\ DNA/CMC, LINE/L1-Tx1) where test support drops below 100 sequences. The most direct remedies are therefore at the data level: a higher minimum-sample threshold for inclusion, hierarchical evaluation that weights the top-level head more heavily for rare superfamilies, or explicit modelling of the label as soft.

\subsection{Interpretability: Saliency and Unsupervised Structure}

Two interpretability analyses point in the same direction. Occlusion-based saliency on individual v4.3 misclassified sequences (Figure~\ref{fig:saliency_analysis}) shows that within-class superfamily confusions (e.g.\ LTR/Gypsy$\leftrightarrow$LTR/Pao) are typically supported by overlapping true- and predicted-class attribution envelopes, while across-class or subfamily-level errors show diffuse or broadly negative true-class saliency. These observations are qualitative and limited to four illustrative examples; an aggregated correct-vs-misclassified comparison was not performed and the broader claim that misclassified sequences systematically lack canonical boundary saliency therefore remains a hypothesis.

Semi-supervised contrastive learning (v5) trained on the same encoder recovers the top-level class structure even when the contrastive objective alone drives the clustering: the UMAP projection separates DNA, LTR and LINE clusters. Within each class, agglomerative hierarchical clustering yields 252 subclusters with modest but non-zero correspondence to annotated superfamilies (ARI~=~0.036, NMI~=~0.136, homogeneity~=~0.247, completeness~=~0.094): individual clusters tend to be superfamily-specific, but each superfamily is fragmented across many clusters. Two practical implications follow. First, the strong semi-supervised signal supports the view that mechanistic class identity is largely recoverable from nucleotide composition alone, complementing the $k$-mer logistic-regression result of Section~\ref{ssec:fullset}. Second, the within-class subclusters that do not map cleanly to a single annotated superfamily are candidate targets for curation: they may reflect sequence subvariants, degenerate copies, chimeric assemblies, or genuinely under-annotated subfamilies finer-grained than the current Pantera labels, and unsupervised mining of the embedding space gives a tractable way to surface them for expert review.

\subsection{Limitations and Future Directions}\label{ssec:limitations}

The most concrete limitation of the present work is at the architectural level. All models from v1 to v4.3 share an auxiliary boundary-prediction head that reads from the masked global average pool; in v1 this head is the primary task, while in v3 and v4.x it is a secondary regulariser. In all cases the head is by construction position-invariant and trained against per-epoch random placement on the canvas. As reported in Section~\ref{ssec:tir}, this recovers sequence \emph{length} well (Pearson $r \approx 0.94$) but is essentially uninformative about absolute start and end positions ($r \approx 0.07$--$0.18$). The failure is structural, not statistical: the head literally cannot see where on the canvas the sequence sits. An in-progress v4.4 variant addresses both root causes simultaneously by (i) appending a small bank of sinusoidal positional channels to the one-hot input, so that absolute canvas position becomes available to the convolutional layers, and (ii) replacing the pooled regression head with a per-position $1\!\times\!1$ convolution that produces a sequence-vs-padding logit at every spatial location and is trained with binary cross-entropy against the downsampled canvas mask. The model has been written but not yet retrained at scale; if it improves boundary localisation without harming classification, it is the natural replacement for v4.3 as the final-form encoder.

Beyond v4.4, three broader directions seem worth exploring. The cross-species generalisation gap motivates either explicit phylogenetic stratification of the training set or domain-adaptation methods that learn lineage-agnostic features. The asymmetry between class-level and superfamily-level performance suggests that bringing in protein-level information --- e.g.\ transposase or reverse-transcriptase domain features for the autonomous elements --- could disambiguate superfamilies that share nucleotide composition but differ in their encoded enzymes. Finally, the unsupervised clustering results raise the question of whether DNA-sequence foundation models, pre-trained at scale and then fine-tuned on Pantera, would provide a stronger encoder than the bespoke tower CNN used here; preliminary attempts in this direction were not pursued in the present project but would be a natural next step.
