\section*{Summary}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Summary}

Transposable elements (TEs) are mobile DNA sequences that occupy a substantial fraction of every vertebrate genome. Their accurate annotation underpins comparative genomics and the study of genome evolution, but existing tools rely on alignment to curated reference libraries and on manual curation, which scale poorly with the volume of data now produced by long-read assembly projects and degrade on divergent or lineage-specific families. This project develops deep-learning classifiers trained directly on the Pantera catalogue of approximately 135{,}000 TE sequences from the Vertebrate Genomes Project (VGP), and asks how far convolutional and graph-based representations of raw DNA sequence can replace alignment as the basis for TE classification.

The work is structured as a three-stage narrative organized around the evaluation design rather than the model architecture. Stage 1 establishes that the Pantera labels carry a strong learnable signal: a tower CNN (v3) and a hybrid CNN+GNN (v4) both achieve high accuracy on a sequence-level hold-out, and a three-class extension (v4.1) further validates label consistency by identifying systematic misclassifications as candidate label noise rather than model error. Stage 2 introduces a species-disjoint outer split and an independent mini-benchmark of three amniote genomes (zebra finch, platypus, American alligator), making cross-species generalization directly measurable for the first time. Stage 3 tightens the pipeline further by fixing the inner validation fold, ensuring that train and validation species are disjoint throughout training; this produces the most rigorously controlled estimates and reveals how much of the Stage-2 superfamily performance was attributable to the split design rather than the architecture.

Two cross-cutting findings emerge from this progression. First, the primary source of cross-species performance variation is species-shift rather than classical overfitting: the encoder generalizes well within the Pantera distribution but shows partial transfer to the amniote benchmark, with the degree of transfer varying by TE class and genome. Disentangling this from the split-design artefact is the central methodological contribution of Stages 2 and 3. Occlusion saliency and semi-supervised contrastive clustering (v5) are reported as qualitative probes of the encoder's internal representations, confirming that the learned embedding organizes TE families in geometrically meaningful ways.

\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Transposable Elements}

% AUDIT(L,C) [L1,C4]: narrowed vertebrate-fraction claim to human; added
% qualifier for other vertebrates.
Transposable elements (TEs) are mobile DNA sequences that propagate copies of themselves within their host genome \cite{mcclintock1950,wells2020field}. Eukaryotic genomes are extensively colonised by TEs: they constitute roughly half of the human genome, with fractions ranging from under 10\% to over 80\% across other vertebrates \cite{wells2020field}, and contribute to chromosomal architecture, gene regulation and species-specific adaptation. Some TE families are autonomous, encoding the enzymes (transposase or reverse transcriptase) required for their own mobilisation; others are non-autonomous, parasitising the machinery of an active autonomous driver \cite{wicker2007unified,wells2020field}.

Knowing which TE families are present in a newly assembled genome, and which copies belong to which family, is a prerequisite for almost every downstream genomic analysis: variant interpretation, comparative genomics, and the study of TE--host coevolution all rely on accurate TE annotation. Existing tools rely on alignment to curated reference libraries together with hand-tuned feature detection and manual curation \cite{smit2015repeatmasker,flynn2020repeatmodeler2}. These approaches scale poorly with the volume of data now produced by long-read assembly projects, and they perform especially badly on divergent or novel families and on non-autonomous elements that are difficult to assign to a driver. A learned classifier trained directly on a large, alignment-independent catalogue would therefore be a valuable complement to alignment-based pipelines.

\subsection{Sequence Features of TE Classes}

The classification task is structured by the biology of how each TE class moves \cite{wicker2007unified,wells2020field}. DNA transposons (Class~II) move by a cut-and-paste mechanism, with autonomous members encoding a transposase that recognises terminal inverted repeats (TIRs) at the boundaries of the element. TIRs are typically 10--50~bp \cite{wicker2007unified}, near-perfect reverse complements of one another, forming a structural signature that a sequence classifier can exploit locally. Class~I elements move via an RNA intermediate. LTR retrotransposons are flanked by direct long terminal repeats (typically 200--600~bp) \cite{wicker2007unified} carrying the regulatory elements for transcription, and internally encode a polyprotein with reverse transcriptase and integrase domains. Long interspersed nuclear elements (LINEs) lack LTRs and instead end with a poly(A) tail; they replicate by target-primed reverse transcription and the autonomous members encode an endonuclease together with a reverse transcriptase.

Two structural features of TE sequences directly constrain the architecture of any classifier. First, a TE may be sequenced from either strand, so a classifier should produce the same prediction for a sequence and its reverse complement (RC). This RC symmetry is a hard constraint that is most cleanly imposed inside the model rather than via test-time augmentation. Second, a TIR is by definition a reverse-complement match between the two ends of the element \cite{wicker2007unified}; detecting it requires comparing positions that may be several kilobases apart in a sequence whose total length varies by two orders of magnitude across families.

\subsection{Previous Work and the Pantera Catalogue}

The most widely used tool for TE annotation, RepeatMasker \cite{smit2015repeatmasker}, identifies repeats by aligning a query genome against a curated library such as Dfam \cite{storer2021dfam} or Repbase. Its output is therefore only as good as the underlying library: divergent or lineage-specific families are missed or systematically misclassified. Several recent efforts have applied machine learning to TE detection or sub-classification, including deep-learning classifiers such as DeepTE \cite{yan2020deepte} and TERL \cite{requena2021terl}; however, most have either focused on a single TE class or been trained on the same curated reference libraries they are meant to extend, limiting their ability to generalise to novel or lineage-specific families.

The starting point for this project is the Pantera catalogue, which uses pangenome polymorphisms to detect TE insertions that are absent in some haplotypes of a species \cite{sierra2024pantera}. Applied to the Vertebrate Genomes Project (VGP) assemblies \cite{rhie2021vgp}, Pantera produces a catalogue of approximately 135{,}000 sequences from over fifty vertebrate species. Because Pantera detects elements directly from polymorphism, it provides an alignment-independent training set on which a learned classifier can be built without circularly inheriting the biases of a reference library. 

\subsection{Convolutional Neural Networks for Sequence Classification}

Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) \cite{lecun2015deep,krizhevsky2012imagenet,alipanahi2015deepbind} are a natural choice for detecting short, position-invariant motifs in biological sequences, and are the fundament of the classifiers in this thesis. A 1-D convolution over a one-hot-encoded sequence learns a bank of filters whose activations indicate where in the sequence each motif occurs; stacking convolutions with non-linearities then composes these motifs into longer patterns. Further, dilated convolutions enlarge the receptive field exponentially with depth without an equivalent growth in parameters \cite{yu2016dilated}, which is necessary to span TIRs whose two halves may be several kilobases apart.

CNNs also have well-known limitations. After global pooling, all positional information is averaged out, so a CNN-only classifier cannot directly use absolute or relative position within the sequence. Within the receptive field of a given filter, the precise spatial layout of nucleotides is preserved, but anything more global must be re-learned from indirect cues such as motif density.

\subsection{Graph Neural Networks for Compositional Information}\label{ssec:gnn_intro}

A complementary view of a TE sequence is its $k$-mer composition: the multiset of all length-$k$ substrings, which captures global compositional bias and many long-range correlations that motif filters do not see. Even a logistic regression on canonical 7-mer frequencies separates the three top-level classes (DNA, LTR, LINE) at roughly 85\% macro $F_1$ (Section~\ref{ssec:stage2}; Figure~\ref{fig:kmer_separation}), establishing that compositional signal alone is highly informative for class-level identification. However, a single global $k$-mer histogram discards positional structure entirely, and a fixed positional binning would tie the representation to a particular sequence length.

A graph neural network (GNN) \cite{kipf2016semi,hamilton2017inductive,zhou2019graph} provides a flexible compromise. The sequence is broken into overlapping windows; each window contributes a node whose feature vector is its windowed $k$-mer profile, and adjacent windows are connected by edges. Message passing then mixes information between neighbouring windows over several layers, producing a global representation that retains coarse positional structure without requiring fixed-length input. Using canonical $k$-mers (the lexicographically smaller of a $k$-mer and its reverse complement) keeps the representation strand-symmetric.

\subsection{Attention Fusion and Hierarchical Heads}

CNN and GNN branches see complementary aspects of the same sequence: the CNN captures local motifs, the GNN focuses on windowed composition. An attention-style gating layer \cite{vaswani2017attention} learns a per-input convex combination of the two branch embeddings, so that the fused representation can lean on whichever modality is locally more informative. The downstream classifier is hierarchical: a top-level head (binary DNA/non-DNA, or three-way DNA/LTR/LINE) is paired with a superfamily head, mirroring both the biological structure and the empirical observation that top-level discrimination is intrinsically easier than fine-grained superfamily assignment.

\subsection{Model Intuition and Stages}\label{ssec:model_stages}

The work conducted in this thesis can be viewed as three stages, each with a different focus and set of experiments, but all building on the same core architecture and training pipeline. 

The first stage is the development of machine-learned classifiers to validate the existing Pantera catalogue, including its class labels and superfamily labels. This starts with a proof-of-concept CNN-only model for TIR detection. Then the model was extended over the whole dataset,  with a specific focus on classification within DNA transposons with a more complex architecture (v3); and then the GNN branch was added to create hybrid models (v4). The model is further extended to a three-class version (v4.1) to leverage the richer supervision signal in the full class-label dataset, which is expected to validate all major classes (DNA, LTR, LINE) and their superfamilies. 

In the second stage, the focus shifts to measuring how well the model generalises to new species. Three amniote genomes (zebra finch, platypus, American alligator) are excised entirely as an independent mini-benchmark, and the outer data split is reorganised at the species level. Retraining the three-class hybrid under this protocol produces v4.2 and v4.3, whose cross-species performance can be directly compared to Stage~1.

In the third stage the inner validation fold is also fixed at the species level, ensuring that training and validation species are disjoint for the entire run. This v4.3-singlefold model provides the most conservative and interpretable generalisation estimate of the project, and its results are combined with the mini-benchmark to characterise how much of the apparent Stage-2 gain was attributable to the evaluation design.

\subsection{Sequence Representation}\label{ssec:repr}

All models take one-hot encoded DNA sequences as input. Each nucleotide is mapped to one of five channels (A, C, G, T, N), where N covers ambiguous or non-standard bases. Channels are stacked along the feature dimension to produce a tensor of shape (5, $L$). 

Because CNNs require a fixed input length, every sequence is placed on a fixed-length canvas (20~kb by default). In each epoch, the start and end positions are sampled uniformly, and the rest of the canvas is filled with zero columns. A binary mask records which positions carry real sequence, which is consumed by the masked global pooling layer so that padded positions do not contribute to the pooled embedding. Random placement during training also serves as positional augmentation, where the model sees each sequence at many different absolute offsets across epochs.

Reverse-complement invariance is built into the encoding. The reverse complement of a one-hot tensor is obtained by reversing the spatial axis and remapping channels (A$\leftrightarrow$T, C$\leftrightarrow$G); this is a deterministic operation that adds no new parameters. Both the forward and the reverse-complement views of a sequence are passed through the encoder during the forward pass and fused for later embeddings (Section~\ref{ssec:methods_family}).

\subsection{Introductory Model: TIR Detection CNN}\label{ssec:methods_v1}

The introductory task, v1 model, is a binary classifier for TIR presence and serves as a proof of concept that the encoder family used in subsequent models can detect a structural TE feature directly from raw sequence. TIR are purely sequence-based feature, and therefore the TIR detection model is purely based on CNN. 

The CNN encoder uses three parallel branches (kernel widths 7, 15, 21~bp; 128 feature maps each), concatenated and mixed by a $1\!\times\!1$ convolution, then followed by four dilated convolutional blocks (kernel size 9; dilation rates 1, 2, 4, 8; GELU, batch normalisation, dropout 0.15, residual). A masked global average pool produces a 128-dimensional embedding. RC invariance is achieved by late fusion: both the forward and RC-strand embeddings are averaged before the binary head. 

\subsection{Classification Model Family}\label{ssec:methods_family}

The TIR detector (v1) above and the classifiers used at every stage below share the same encoder backbone and a small set of optional extensions. This subsection describes the family once; the differences between v3, v4, v4.1, v4.3 and v4.3-singlefold are then differences only in (i) the head (binary vs three-way; 7 vs 23 superfamilies), (ii) the data split (Section~\ref{ssec:splits}), and in two cases (iii) the presence of the GNN branch.

\paragraph{CNN tower (v3 onward).} The pooled 128-dim embedding from the v1 encoder (Section~\ref{ssec:methods_v1}) is fed into a hierarchical pair of heads: a top-level head (binary DNA vs non-DNA in v3 and v4; three-way DNA/LTR/LINE in v4.1 onward) and a superfamily head, jointly trained with the superfamily loss masked out for examples whose top-level label falls outside the superfamily vocabulary.

\paragraph{Hybrid CNN+GNN (v4 onward).} The v4 hybrid model adds a parallel $k$-mer GNN branch alongside the CNN tower. The sequence is tiled into 512~bp windows with stride 256~bp (50\% overlap); each window is represented by its canonical 7-mer histogram (canonical $k$-mers being the lexicographically smaller of a $k$-mer and its reverse complement, which keeps the representation strand-symmetric), hashed into a fixed 2048-dim feature vector, and concatenated with a short relative positional encoding. The windows form the nodes of a chain graph with bidirectional edges between adjacent windows and self-loops; a 3-layer GraphSAGE-style \cite{hamilton2017inductive} GNN with hidden dimension 128 then aggregates information across neighbouring windows, and a scatter-mean pool produces a single 128-dim graph-level embedding. The CNN and GNN embeddings are fused by an attention gate: a small MLP takes the concatenation of the two and outputs a two-element softmax, giving a per-input convex combination of the two branches. The fused embedding is then fed to the same hierarchical heads as v3.

\paragraph{Three-class extensions (v4.1, v4.3, v4.3-singlefold).} The hybrid architecture is unchanged; only the top-level head is broadened from binary to three-way (DNA / LTR / LINE) and the superfamily head from 7 to 23 outputs. Sequences with rare or ambiguous classes (SINE 0.42\%, PLE 0.20\%, RC 0.12\%) and unannotated sequences are excluded from training and evaluation. 

\paragraph{Boundary-prediction auxiliary head.} A two-layer MLP (128$\to$64$\to$3, GELU, sigmoid) on the pooled embedding predicts the normalised start, end and length of the real sequence on the canvas, trained with MSE at loss-weight 0.1. It acts as a regulariser by encouraging the encoder to retain enough spatial information to recover sequence placement; as discussed in Section~\ref{ssec:tir} and Section~\ref{ssec:limitations}, length is recoverable but absolute canvas position is not.

\paragraph{Contrastive embedding head (v5).} For the semi-supervised clustering analysis in Section~\ref{ssec:clustering}, the pretrained v4 hybrid encoder is reused: the classification head is replaced by a 128-dim $\ell_2$-normalised projection head, and the encoder is fine-tuned with a combined loss
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{SupCon}} + \lambda_{\text{cls}} \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}} + \lambda_{\text{ctr}} \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{center}}
\]
where $\mathcal{L}_{\text{SupCon}}$ is the supervised contrastive loss \cite{khosla2020supervised} ($\tau = 0.3$), $\mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}}$ is cross-entropy over the DNA superfamilies that anchors the embedding to the known label structure and prevents collapse, and $\mathcal{L}_{\text{center}}$ pulls embeddings toward learned per-class centroids ($\lambda_{\text{cls}} = 0.5$, $\lambda_{\text{ctr}} = 0.01$). After training, 256-dim fused embeddings are projected to 2-D with UMAP and clustered with HDBSCAN.

\paragraph{Stage 1 --- dataset validation (v3, v4, v4.1).} All data splitting are performed on the sequence level. For v3 and v4 focusing on DNA transposons, since DNA only accounts for 7.9\% of the full dataset, the training pool is balanced by subsampling non-DNA sequences to 20{,}000 as negatives. For DNA sequences, the superfamilies with fewer than 100 examples are excluded to ensure a minimum amount of data for training, resulting in a pool of 20{,}489 DNA sequences and therefore a total of 40{,}489 sequences. The train-test split is performed at the sequence level with a ratio of 80:20, and then train and val are formed by a 5-fold StratifiedKFold on the training pool, with the active val fold cycled per epoch. 

The three-way models (v4.1 and onwards) are trained on  LTR, LINE, and DNA, which sum up to 36.1\% of the whole dataset. The remaining classes are too rare to include and unannotated sequences are also excluded. A similar superfamily-level filter with lower cap at 100 samples and upper cap at 5000 samples is applied to balance the dataset, resulting a pool of 49{,}832 sequences. The same sequence-level train-test split and inner CV structure are applied as in the binary case. 

This split allows species to appear in both train and test, so high test scores are inflated relative to a species-stratified or naturally distributed evaluation. The intended use of Stage~1 is therefore not to estimate generalisation but to perform a label validation: under this split a learned classifier should reproduce the labels almost everywhere if those labels carry any consistent signal, and the residual misclassifications then become candidates for relabelling or curation. This is the same logic used in confident-learning approaches to dataset cleaning \cite{northcutt2021confident}, and in the established practice within RepeatMasker / Dfam pipelines of cycling self-classification through curation \cite{storer2021dfam,flynn2020repeatmodeler2}.

\paragraph{Stage 2 --- species-disjoint outer split (v4.2, v4.3).} We recognize the species level data leaking in stage 1. Hence in stage 2, the dataset is shuffled at species level as a species-disjoint test set, and three additional species (\textit{T.~guttata}, \textit{O.~anatinus}, \textit{A.~mississippiensis}) are excised entirely as the mini-benchmark (Section~\ref{ssec:mini_benchmark}) for v4.3 model. Inside the remaining 406 trainval species, a 5-fold straitified shuffle rotates the val fold each epoch. Within any single epoch train and val are species-disjoint, but across the run every val species rotates into training, so val metrics inflate to near-perfect by late epochs. The test set is unaffected because the test species were excised before any rotation began. 

\paragraph{Stage 3 --- single-fold inner split (v4.3-singlefold).} The outer test split is identical to Stage~2. The rotating inner CV is replaced by a single fixed species-level shuffle, so train and val species are disjoint for the whole run and the val metrics are no longer inflated by the inner-CV rotation. This is the most stringent split used in the project and provides the most conservative estimate of generalisation to truly unseen species when combined with the mini-benchmark.

\subsection{Training, Evaluation and Implementation}\label{ssec:training}

All models are trained with cross-entropy losses (label smoothing $\alpha = 0.1$ on multi-class heads; inverse square-root class weighting on the superfamily head) and the boundary-regression auxiliary loss at weight 0.1. Training uses early stopping on validation macro $F_1$ with patience 10 epochs, with the additional cap of 40 epochs to limit the overfitting documented in Section~\ref{ssec:stage1}. Batch size, optimiser, learning rate and schedule details are listed in the hyperparameter table (Table~\ref{tab:hyperparams}). 

Three test sets recur throughout the Results, with the assignment determined by stage. Reported metrics are accuracy, macro and weighted $F_1$, and per-class $F_1$; AUROC and AUPRC for binary tasks; and separate top-level and superfamily scores for hierarchical models. Per-superfamily $F_1$ is reported only for superfamilies with $\geq$30 test samples. For the mini-benchmark, FASTA headers are mapped to the model vocabulary by exact superfamily match, parent-class fallback, or exclusion; sequences outside the three-way head (SINE, RC/Helitron, Unknown) are excluded.

All models were implemented in PyTorch 2.0+ and trained either locally on Apple-silicon MPS or on NVIDIA A100 GPU on CSD3 (Cambridge Service for Data Driven Discovery).

As a proof of concept, the v1 CNN (Section~\ref{ssec:methods_v1}) was trained for binary TIR-presence detection. After stratified subsampling to a balanced training split, the model achieves 86\% accuracy and macro $F_1 = 0.86$ (TIR-positive $F_1 = 0.85$, TIR-negative $F_1 = 0.86$). \subsection{Stage 2 --- Extending to the Species-Disjoint Protocol}\label{ssec:stage2}

\subsubsection{Protocol and Train/Val Leak Caveat}

Stage~2 retrains the same hybrid architecture under the protocol of Section~\ref{ssec:splits}. In particular, the v4.2 model use a lower superfamily level upper cap at 3{,}000 sequences instead of 5{,}000 to test the model performance with more balanced but also less abundant data. Two checkpoints are reported: epoch 28 on v4.2 model (not excluding the three mini-benchmark species) and epoch 40 onv4.3. The inner rotating CV cycles every val species through training across the run, so the val metrics inflate to $\approx$1.0 by late epochs and become unreliable for early stopping. The Stage-3 single-fold inner split (Section~\ref{ssec:stage3}) addresses this. 

\subsubsection{Performance on Species-Disjoint Pantera Test}

On the species-disjoint Pantera test set, v4.3 reaches three-class macro $F_1 = 0.982$ (accuracy 98.4\%) and superfamily macro $F_1 = 0.727$ over 23 superfamilies; v4.2 reaches class $F_1 = 0.895$ / sf $F_1 = 0.604$. All three top-level classes are well separated under v4.3 (DNA $F_1 = 0.978$, LTR $0.990$, LINE $0.978$). Per-superfamily $F_1$ on the test split is highest for LINE families (LINE/L1 $0.96$, LINE/CR1 $\approx 0.99$) and for the largest DNA superfamily (DNA/hAT $0.92$); the generic LTR catch-all label remains the hardest single superfamily ($F_1 \approx 0.33$). The 0.92 DNA/hAT score is, however, qualified by Stage~3 (Section~\ref{ssec:stage3}): once the inner train/val rotation is removed, this score collapses to $\approx 0.26$, so the Stage-2 hAT number is best recognized as an upper bound that depended on the contaminated validation set rather than as a stable test-set result.

\subsection{Stage 3 --- Single-Fold Inner Split (v4.3-singlefold)}\label{ssec:stage3}

\subsubsection{Removing the Train/Val Species Overlap}

Stage~3 keeps the same outer species-disjoint test split as Stage~2 but replaces the rotating 5-fold inner CV with a single fixed shuffle on the 406 trainval species, as stated in Section~\ref{ssec:splits}. Train and val species are now disjoint for the entire run, so the val metrics are no longer inflated by the rotating-CV schedule, and validation superfamily $F_1$ plateaus at $\approx 0.67$--$0.70$ rather than 0.99. The best v4.3-singlefold checkpoint is epoch 28.

\subsubsection{Comparison on model performance}

On the same species-disjoint Pantera test set used in Stage~2, v4.3-singlefold reaches class macro $F_1 = 0.955$ (accuracy 95.9\%) and superfamily macro $F_1 = 0.638$. A detailed comparison to the Stage~1 and Stage~2 results is conducted in Table~\ref{tab:stage_compare}. 

\paragraph{The DNA/hAT collapse.} In particular, DNA/hAT shows two large and mechanistically distinct drops that together are the dominant contributor to the $\Delta$~sf macro $F_1 = 0.089$ between Stages~2 and~3. First, between v4.2 ($F_1 = 0.265$) and v4.3 rotating ($F_1 = 0.919$), while the models share the same architecture, the driver is the superfamily cap: at cap~$= 3{,}000$, DNA/hAT is the only superfamily in the corpus with more than 3{,}000 sequences (4{,}900 total). For superfamilies with high internal diversity like hAt, this reduction is fatel on the model performance; on the other hand, the other three capped superfamilies (LTR/Gypsy, LINE/L1, LTR/DIRS) have way more than 5{,}000 sequences but are achieving around 0.90 $F_1$ in both scenarios. 

The second gap, from v4.3 rotating ($F_1 = 0.919$) to v4.3-singlefold ($F_1 = 0.256$), is harder to explain. The only protocol change is removing the rotating inner CV, but attributing the collapse solely to train/val species overlap is not straightforward: the three other capped superfamilies (LTR/Gypsy, LINE/L1, LTR/DIRS) lose only 1--5 percentage points under the same protocol change, whereas hAT loses 0.663 points, pointing to a distinct and as-yet-unexplained vulnerability specific to hAT. A candidate explanation is phylogenetic coverage: the outer split concentrates species which carry genomically distinct hAT variants, in the test fold with no phylogenetic neighbours in the training species. The exact mechanism, however, remains an open question (Section~\ref{ssec:limitations}).

\subsection{Saliency as a Sanity Check}\label{ssec:saliency}

Gradient-based saliency \cite{simonyan2014deep} serves as a qualitative check on which sequence regions drive predictions, not as causal attribution. For each test sequence, the $\ell_2$-normalised input gradient $\nabla_x L$ is computed; positive values flag positions that increase the true-class logit. The figure is generated with v4 model, but is still representitive since occlusion saliency on a single sequence asks which positions drive that prediction, and is invariant to which species were in the training fold. 

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{saliency_analysis.png}
\caption{Occlusion-based saliency for four representative misclassified test sequences from the dominant within-class superfamily confusion pairs (DNA/hAT$\rightarrow$DNA, LTR/Gypsy$\rightarrow$LTR/Pao, DNA$\rightarrow$DNA/hAT, LINE/L1$\rightarrow$LINE/L1-Tx1) identified on the v4. For each example, two curves show the per-position drop in the true-class logit (blue) and in the predicted-class logit (orange) when a sliding window is masked, so positive values mark regions important for the corresponding class. A confident error like LINE/L1$\rightarrow$LINE/L1-Tx1 typically shows broadly negative true-class saliency across the body, while ambiguous within-class errors (LTR/Gypsy$\leftrightarrow$LTR/Pao) show overlapping true- and predicted-class envelopes consistent with shared internal motifs.}

\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:saliency_analysis} examines four representative misclassified sequences. \section{Discussion}

\subsection{The Three-Stage Lesson: Splits, Not Architectures}\label{ssec:disc_stages}

The single clearest finding of the project is that, holding the hybrid architecture fixed, the choice of data-split protocol changes the headline numbers by far more than any modelling decision did. Stage~1 (random hold-out) gave the most flattering numbers (v4.1 three-class test $F_1 = 0.984$, sf $F_1 = 0.872$) but with a 0.13 val/test gap that was already a quantitative warning of species leakage. Stage~2 (species-disjoint outer split with rotating-CV inner) gave the best species-disjoint Pantera test numbers (v4.3 class $F_1 = 0.982$, sf $F_1 = 0.727$) but inflated the validation signal, where val sf $F_1$ reached $\approx 1.0$ by late epochs due to each fold's val species were recycled back into training; the price of relying on this val for early stopping was the $0.92$ DNA/hAT score, which collapsed to $0.26$ once the rotation was removed. Stage~3 (single-fold inner split) is the only setting in which val and test agree (val sf $F_1 \approx 0.70$ vs test $0.638$). 

The pattern is not specific to this thesis: in any species-grouped sequence-classification problem, a rotating-CV inner schedule combined with a species-grouped outer test will produce a clean test set but a contaminated validation signal as in Stage~2 and the $0.092$ residual sf-$F_1$ gap to Stage~3 should be expected as the cost of repairing it. The three-stage progression also suggests a re-phrasing of Stage~1: rather than reporting it as ``the in-distribution baseline'', it is more useful to report it as a label-quality audit \cite{northcutt2021confident} in the spirit of confident-learning and of established RepeatMasker / Dfam curation cycles \cite{storer2021dfam,flynn2020repeatmodeler2}.

\subsection{Species-Shift as the Primary Source of Cross-Species Performance Variation}\label{ssec:disc_species_shift}

The first insight from the result of Stage~1 alone would be recognizing the val/test gap as classical overfitting and prescribe more regularisation, more data augmentation or smaller models. The observations from Stages~2 and~3 mini-benchmark results (Section~\ref{ssec:mini_benchmark}) actually reveal that the dominant failure mode in this project is species-shift. First, when the rotating-CV inner schedule is removed in Stage~3, the species-disjoint Pantera test sf $F_1$ drops by $0.089$ but the mini-benchmark sf $F_1$ rises slightly ($0.231 \to 0.250$); training under the rotating CV inflates in-distribution numbers without buying any out-of-distribution generalisation. Second, the per-class transfer profile (Table~\ref{tab:mini_benchmark_perclass}) is strongly lineage-dependent under both stages: platypus generalises best, alligator second, zebra finch worst, with DNA $F_1$ in zebra finch falling to $0.40$ and LTR $F_1$ collapsing to $0.21$--$0.50$ across all three species. The avian-specific ERV families that dominate zebra finch LTR mass lack close analogues in the trainval pool. Third, the DNA/hAT collapse documented in Section~\ref{ssec:stage3} is shared by both leak-controlled variants, suggesting the limit there is not val-set memorisation but a genuine inability of a 23-way flat softmax over consensus sequence to separate hAT subvariants from a heterogeneous generic-DNA catch-all. The most productive directions are broader phylogenetic training coverage, domain-adversarial training \cite{ganin2015unsupervised}, or pre-training on a much larger DNA corpus before fine-tuning on Pantera; meta-learning across species \cite{finn2017model} would be a complementary direction for low-shot lineages.

\subsection{When the GNN Branch Helps}\label{ssec:hybrid_disc}

The GNN branch contributes a clear improvement on the top-level task, as seen from binary v4 (Section~\ref{ssec:hybrid}) and three-way v4.3 (Section~\ref{ssec:stage2}), but not on within-class superfamily discrimination. The attention gate is highly variable in the binary setting and converges to GNN weight $\approx 0.72$ from $\approx$ epoch 25 in the three-way setting, exactly tracking the standalone $k$-mer baseline. The gate is best read as a soft prior on which modality to trust: the GNN sees global composition, which is informative for top-level mechanism but not for the local motif differences that distinguish superfamilies, and the gate downweights it when the head asks a within-class question. 

\subsection{The Boundary-Prediction Diagnostic}\label{ssec:disc_boundary}

The most reproducible structural observation across all v1--v4.3 runs is from the auxiliary boundary-regression head. Sequence length is recovered well ($r \approx 0.94$) but absolute canvas position is not ($r \approx 0.07$--$0.18$). This is not a hyperparameter pathology but a consequence of reading regression targets from a position-invariant masked global average pool, after which absolute placement information has been pooled away. The diagnostic is mainly useful as a design constraint for the v4.4 successor sketched in Section~\ref{ssec:limitations}: any model that needs to localise TE boundaries on a long canvas must inject a positional channel (sinusoidal or learned) before the pooling step, and probably needs a per-position segmentation head rather than a regression head reading from a pooled embedding.

\subsection{Limitations and Future Directions}\label{ssec:limitations}

\paragraph{Stage 1 is a label audit, not a generalisation estimate.} The Stage-1 hold-out is sequence-level random with class-balancing applied before the split (Section~\ref{ssec:splits}). Its test $F_1$ is therefore inflated for rare classes, and its primary value is as a label-quality check rather than a generalisation estimate. The Stage-3 mini-benchmark (Section~\ref{ssec:mini_benchmark}) provides the most realistic out-of-distribution numbers in the project.

\paragraph{v4.4: a position-aware successor.} The boundary-prediction diagnostic of Section~\ref{ssec:disc_boundary} motivates an in-progress v4.4 variant that adds sinusoidal positional channels to the input and replaces the regression head with a per-position segmentation head. If it improves localisation without harming classification, it is the natural successor to v4.3.

\paragraph{Beyond v4.4.} There are two directions follow naturally from the species-shift framing of Section~\ref{ssec:disc_species_shift}: (i)~broader phylogenetic training coverage or domain-adversarial learning \cite{ganin2015unsupervised} to close the cross-species gap; (ii)~DNA-sequence foundation models pre-trained at scale and fine-tuned on Pantera. Confident-learning style relabelling \cite{northcutt2021confident} of the Stage-1 disagreements would also tighten the underlying ground truth for any of these directions.
