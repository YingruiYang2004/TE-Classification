\section*{Summary}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Summary}

Transposable elements (TEs) are mobile DNA sequences that occupy a substantial fraction of every vertebrate genome. Their accurate annotation underpins comparative genomics and the study of genome evolution, but existing tools rely on alignment to curated reference libraries and on manual curation, which scale poorly with the volume of data now produced by long-read assembly projects and degrade on divergent or lineage-specific families. This project develops deep-learning classifiers trained directly on the Pantera catalogue of approximately 145{,}000 TE consensi from the Vertebrate Genomes Project (VGP), and asks how far convolutional and graph-based representations of raw DNA sequence can replace alignment as the basis for TE classification.

Four models are presented in sequence. A reverse-complement-invariant tower CNN with multi-scale and dilated convolutions is first trained for terminal-inverted-repeat (TIR) detection, where careful subsampling outperforms loss-based reweightings. A hierarchical CNN classifier (v3) extends this encoder with joint binary (DNA vs non-DNA) and seven-way DNA-superfamily heads, reaching 91.3\% binary accuracy and 94.4\% superfamily accuracy on a held-out test set. A hybrid CNN+GNN model (v4) adds a parallel windowed canonical-7-mer graph branch fused to the CNN through a learned attention gate; the gate exposes a task-dependent split between local motifs and global composition. Broadening the head to three top-level classes and 23 superfamilies (v4.3), with three species withheld for external benchmarking, gives 98.2\% three-class accuracy (macro $F_1 = 0.981$) and 89.3\% superfamily accuracy ($F_1 = 0.839$).

External evaluation on the three held-out species (zebra finch, platypus, American alligator) shows lineage-dependent domain shift: top-level transfer is reasonable on platypus (73.8\%) and alligator (60.1\%) but collapses on zebra finch (33.3\%), where both LTR (F1~=~0.21) and DNA (F1~=~0.35) class discrimination are substantially degraded. Saliency maps and semi-supervised contrastive clustering serve as exploratory sanity checks, surfacing candidate subclusters within DNA superfamilies that warrant curation.

\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Transposable Elements}

% AUDIT(L,C) [L1,C4]: narrowed vertebrate-fraction claim to human; added
% qualifier for other vertebrates.
Transposable elements (TEs) are mobile DNA sequences that propagate copies of themselves within their host genome \cite{mcclintock1950,wells2020field}. Eukaryotic genomes are extensively colonised by TEs: they constitute roughly half of the human genome, with fractions ranging from under 10\% to over 80\% across other vertebrates \cite{wells2020field}, and contribute to chromosomal architecture, gene regulation and species-specific adaptation. Some TE families are autonomous, encoding the enzymes (transposase or reverse transcriptase) required for their own mobilisation; others are non-autonomous, parasitising the machinery of an active autonomous driver \cite{wicker2007unified,wells2020field}.

Knowing which TE families are present in a newly assembled genome, and which copies belong to which family, is a prerequisite for almost every downstream genomic analysis: variant interpretation, comparative genomics, and the study of TE--host coevolution all rely on accurate TE annotation. Existing tools rely on alignment to curated reference libraries together with hand-tuned feature detection and manual curation \cite{smit2015repeatmasker,flynn2020repeatmodeler2}. These approaches scale poorly with the volume of data now produced by long-read assembly projects, and they perform especially badly on divergent or novel families and on non-autonomous elements that are difficult to assign to a driver. A learned classifier that can be trained directly from large catalogues of unannotated insertions would therefore be a valuable complement to alignment-based pipelines.

\subsection{Sequence Features of TE Classes}

The classification task is structured by the biology of how each TE class moves \cite{wicker2007unified,wells2020field}. DNA transposons (Class~II) move by a cut-and-paste mechanism, with autonomous members encoding a transposase that recognises terminal inverted repeats (TIRs) at the boundaries of the element. TIRs are typically 10--50~bp \cite{wicker2007unified}, near-perfect reverse complements of one another, forming a structural signature that a sequence classifier can exploit locally. Class~I elements move via an RNA intermediate. LTR retrotransposons are flanked by direct long terminal repeats (typically 200--600~bp) \cite{wicker2007unified} carrying the regulatory elements for transcription, and internally encode a polyprotein with reverse transcriptase and integrase domains. Long interspersed nuclear elements (LINEs) lack LTRs and instead end with a poly(A) tail; they replicate by target-primed reverse transcription and the autonomous members encode an endonuclease together with a reverse transcriptase.

Two structural features of TE sequences directly constrain the architecture of any classifier. First, a TE may be sequenced from either strand, so a classifier should produce the same prediction for a sequence and its reverse complement (RC). This RC symmetry is a hard constraint that is most cleanly imposed inside the model rather than via test-time augmentation. Second, a TIR is by definition a reverse-complement match between the two ends of the element \cite{wicker2007unified}; detecting it requires comparing positions that may be several kilobases apart in a sequence whose total length varies by two orders of magnitude across families.

\subsection{Previous Work and the Pantera Catalogue}

The most widely used tool for TE annotation, RepeatMasker \cite{smit2015repeatmasker}, identifies repeats by aligning a query genome against a curated library such as Dfam \cite{storer2021dfam} or Repbase. Its output is therefore only as good as the underlying library: divergent or lineage-specific families are missed or systematically misclassified. Several recent efforts have applied machine learning to TE detection or sub-classification\,---\,including deep-learning classifiers such as DeepTE \cite{yan2020deepte} and TERL \cite{requena2021terl}; however, most have either focused on a single TE class or been trained on the same curated reference libraries they are meant to extend, limiting their ability to generalise to novel or lineage-specific families.

The starting point for this project is the Pantera catalogue, which uses pangenome polymorphisms to detect TE insertions that are absent in some haplotypes of a species \cite{sierra2024pantera}. Applied to the Vertebrate Genomes Project (VGP) assemblies \cite{rhie2021vgp}, Pantera produces a catalogue of approximately 145{,}000 sequences from over fifty vertebrate species, with FASTA-header annotations giving the broad TE class (DNA, LTR, LINE, SINE, RC, \dots) and, where assignable, the superfamily. Because Pantera detects elements directly from polymorphism, it provides an unusually large, alignment-independent training set on which a learned classifier can be built without circularly inheriting the biases of a reference library. The dataset is, however, heavily skewed: roughly 92\% of sequences carry no transposase annotation, DNA transposons constitute only about 8\%, and many superfamilies have fewer than a hundred examples.

\subsection{Convolutional Neural Networks for Sequence Classification}

Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) \cite{lecun2015deep,krizhevsky2012imagenet,alipanahi2015deepbind} are a natural choice for detecting short, position-invariant motifs in biological sequences. A 1-D convolution over a one-hot-encoded sequence learns a bank of filters whose activations indicate where in the sequence each motif occurs; stacking convolutions with non-linearities then composes these motifs into longer patterns. Two architectural ingredients matter for TE sequences in particular. \emph{Multi-scale} convolutions, with several kernel widths in parallel, allow the network to capture both short, locally conserved motifs and longer structural features in the same forward pass. \emph{Dilated} convolutions enlarge the receptive field exponentially with depth without an equivalent growth in parameters \cite{yu2016dilated}, which is necessary to span TIRs whose two halves may be several kilobases apart.

CNNs also have well-known limitations. After global pooling, all positional information is averaged out, so a CNN-only classifier cannot directly use absolute or relative position within the sequence. Within the receptive field of a given filter, the precise spatial layout of nucleotides is preserved, but anything more global must be re-learned from indirect cues such as motif density.

\subsection{Graph Neural Networks for Compositional Information}\label{ssec:gnn_intro}

A complementary view of a TE sequence is its $k$-mer composition: the multiset of all length-$k$ substrings, which captures global compositional bias and many long-range correlations that motif filters do not see. Even a logistic regression on canonical 7-mer frequencies separates the three top-level classes (DNA, LTR, LINE) at roughly 85\% macro $F_1$ (Section~\ref{ssec:fullset}; Figure~\ref{fig:kmer_separation}), establishing that compositional signal alone is highly informative for class-level identification. However, a single global $k$-mer histogram discards positional structure entirely, and a fixed positional binning would tie the representation to a particular sequence length.

A graph neural network (GNN) \cite{kipf2016semi,hamilton2017inductive,zhou2019graph} provides a flexible compromise. The sequence is broken into overlapping windows; each window contributes a node whose feature vector is its windowed $k$-mer profile, and adjacent windows are connected by edges. Message passing then mixes information between neighbouring windows over several layers, producing a global representation that retains coarse positional structure without requiring fixed-length input. Using \emph{canonical} $k$-mers (the lexicographically smaller of a $k$-mer and its reverse complement) keeps the representation strand-symmetric.

\subsection{Attention Fusion and Hierarchical Heads}

CNN and GNN branches see complementary aspects of the same sequence: the CNN sees local motifs, the GNN sees windowed composition. An attention-style gating layer \cite{vaswani2017attention} learns a per-input convex combination of the two branch embeddings, so that the fused representation can lean on whichever modality is locally more informative. The downstream classifier is hierarchical: a top-level head (binary DNA/non-DNA, or three-way DNA/LTR/LINE) is paired with a superfamily head, mirroring both the biological taxonomy and the empirical observation that top-level discrimination is intrinsically easier than fine-grained superfamily assignment.

This thesis develops these ideas through a sequence of models: a TIR-detection CNN (v1), a hierarchical CNN for DNA superfamilies (v3), a hybrid CNN+GNN (v4), a three-class extension evaluated on held-out species (v4.3), and a contrastive-learning embedding for clustering (v5). Full architectural details are in Section~\ref{sec:methods}.

\section{Data and Methods}\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Data}\label{ssec:data}

The training data are transposable element sequences identified by Pantera \cite{sierra2024pantera} from the Vertebrate Genomes Project (VGP) assemblies. Pantera detects TE families directly from pangenome polymorphisms by identifying insertions present in some haplotypes and absent in others, and therefore produces a comprehensive catalogue of TEs without inheriting the family list of any particular reference library. The catalogue used here contains approximately 145{,}000 sequences from over fifty vertebrate species. 

In particular, the dataset includes 11{,}500 TIR-positive sequences that are used to train the v1 TIR-detection model. The full set of sequences, with class and superfamily labels parsed from the FASTA headers, is used for the v3 and v4 models. The v4.3 three-class model and the external benchmark (Section~\ref{ssec:mini_benchmark}) use a subset of the full set with only DNA, LTR and LINE classes.

The class distribution is heavily imbalanced: unannotated sequences (``None'') dominate at 62.3\%, with LTR (18.8\%), LINE (10.4\%) and DNA (7.9\%) as the major labelled classes (Figure~\ref{fig:dataset_distribution}). For the v4.3 three-class model and the external benchmark (Section~\ref{ssec:mini_benchmark}), three species are excluded from \emph{both} training and validation and reserved as a held-out external test set: Zebra finch (\textit{Taeniopygia guttata}), platypus (\textit{Ornithorhynchus anatinus}), and American alligator (\textit{Alligator mississippiensis}). For each of these species, a mini-benchmark dataset was assembled comprising three complementary sources: (i)~Pantera-derived consensus sequences identified in that species' genome, (ii)~an externally curated RepeatMasker-format TE reference library with \texttt{Name\#Class/Subclass} annotations, and (iii)~a set of structurally detected LTR retrotransposons produced by genome-wide alignment of LTR terminal repeats. The evaluation in Section~\ref{ssec:mini_benchmark} uses the curated libraries as the primary ground-truth set; the Pantera consensi and structural LTR sequences are additional reference data accompanying the benchmark.

\subsection{Sequence Representation}\label{ssec:repr}

All models take one-hot encoded DNA sequences as input. Each nucleotide is mapped to one of five channels (A, C, G, T, N), where N covers ambiguous or non-standard bases. Channels are stacked along the feature dimension to produce a tensor of shape (5, $L$). 

Because CNNs require a fixed input length, every sequence is placed on a fixed-length canvas ( 20~kb by default). In each epoch, for a sequence of length $\ell$, a start position $s$ is sampled uniformly from $\{0, 1, \dots, L-\ell\}$, the sequence occupies positions $s$ through $s+\ell-1$, and the rest of the canvas is filled with zero columns. A binary mask records which positions carry real sequence; the mask is consumed by the masked global pooling layer so that padded positions do not contribute to the pooled embedding. Random placement during training also serves as positional augmentation: the model sees each sequence at many different absolute offsets across epochs.

Reverse-complement invariance is built into the encoding. The reverse complement of a one-hot tensor is obtained by reversing the spatial axis and remapping channels (A$\leftrightarrow$T, C$\leftrightarrow$G); this is a deterministic operation that adds no new parameters. Both the forward and the reverse-complement views of a sequence are passed through the encoder during the forward pass and fused for later embeddings (Section~\ref{ssec:methods_v3}).

\subsection{Model v1: TIR Detection CNN}\label{ssec:methods_v1}

The v1 model is a binary classifier for TIR presence. The CNN encoder uses three parallel branches (kernel widths 7, 15, 21~bp; 128 feature maps each), concatenated and mixed by a $1\!\times\!1$ convolution, then followed by four dilated convolutional blocks (kernel size 9; dilation rates 1, 2, 4, 8; GELU, batch normalisation, dropout 0.15, residual). A masked global average pool produces a 128-dimensional embedding. RC invariance is achieved by late fusion: both the forward and RC-strand embeddings are averaged before the binary head.

\subsection{Model v3: Hierarchical CNN Classifier}\label{ssec:methods_v3}

The v3 classifier uses the same encoder as v1 but feeds the pooled embedding into two parallel heads: a binary head (DNA vs non-DNA) and a seven-way superfamily head over the seven DNA superfamilies with $\geq$100 examples. Both heads train jointly, with the superfamily loss masked for non-DNA samples. The non-DNA class is subsampled to 20{,}000 sequences; within-DNA imbalance is handled by inverse square-root class weighting. The v3 training set contains 20{,}000 non-DNA and 30{,}431 DNA sequences (7 superfamilies), with 6{,}141 sequences held out.

\subsection{Model v4: Hybrid CNN + k-mer GNN}\label{ssec:methods_v4}

The v4 hybrid model adds a parallel $k$-mer GNN branch to the v3 encoder. The sequence is tiled into 512~bp windows (stride 256~bp), each represented by its canonical 7-mer histogram (dimension 2048) with a relative positional encoding. The windows form a chain graph; a 3-layer GraphSAGE \cite{hamilton2017inductive} GNN with hidden dimension 128 produces a 128-dimensional graph-level embedding via scatter-mean pooling. A small MLP attention gate fuses the CNN and GNN embeddings via a learned convex combination; the fused embedding feeds the same hierarchical heads as v3. The three-class extensions v4.2 and v4.3 broaden the top-level head to DNA/LTR/LINE (23 superfamilies); v4.3 additionally excludes the three benchmark species and caps training at 40 epochs (Section~\ref{ssec:fullset}).

\subsection{Model v5: Contrastive Embedding}\label{ssec:methods_v5}

The v5 model repurposes the pretrained v4 encoder for semi-supervised clustering of DNA transposons. The classification head is replaced by a 128-dimensional $\ell_2$-normalised projection head; the encoder is fine-tuned on a combined loss:
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{SupCon}} + \lambda_{\text{cls}} \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}} + \lambda_{\text{ctr}} \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{center}}
\]
where $\mathcal{L}_{\text{SupCon}}$ is the supervised contrastive loss \cite{khosla2020supervised} ($\tau = 0.3$), $\mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}}$ anchors the representation to known label structure, and $\mathcal{L}_{\text{center}}$ pulls embeddings toward per-class centroids ($\lambda_{\text{cls}} = 0.5$, $\lambda_{\text{ctr}} = 0.01$). After training, 256-dimensional fused embeddings are extracted, projected to 2-D with UMAP, and clustered with HDBSCAN (Section~\ref{ssec:clustering}).

\subsection{Training}\label{ssec:training}

All models are trained with the AdamW optimiser \cite{loshchilov2019decoupled} ($\mathrm{lr} = 10^{-3}$, weight decay $10^{-4}$) and a cosine annealing schedule. Class imbalance is addressed at two levels, as described above: subsampling for the binary task, and inverse square-root class weighting for the superfamily task. Multi-class heads use label smoothing ($\alpha = 0.1$).

The CNN-only models use batch size 32; the hybrid model uses batch size 16 because of the additional memory required for the graph branch. Note that this difference in batch size is a potential confound when comparing v3 and v4 performance directly. Training uses early stopping with patience of 10 epochs based on the validation macro $F_1$. Five-fold cross-validation was used during model development to guide hyperparameter selection; all performance figures reported in the Results are single-split test-set point estimates on the held-out species-stratified split (80\%/20\%); no standard errors are available for the neural model test scores.

An auxiliary boundary regression head (two-layer MLP, MSE loss weighted at 0.1) predicts the normalised start, end and length of the sequence on the canvas, acting as a regulariser. Length predictions are accurate ($r \approx 0.94$) but absolute canvas positions are not ($r \approx 0.07$--$0.18$), for the structural reason discussed in Section~\ref{ssec:limitations}.

\textbf{Split and balancing note.} The split unit in all v1--v4.3 models is the individual sequence (not species), and class-balancing is applied \emph{before} the 80\%/20\% split. The test set therefore inherits the artificially balanced distribution, inflating per-class $F_1$ relative to a species-stratified or naturally distributed evaluation. The external benchmark (Section~\ref{ssec:mini_benchmark}), evaluated on unmodified curated libraries, is not subject to this artefact and provides the more conservative estimate of generalisation.

\subsection{Evaluation}\label{ssec:evaluation}

Metrics reported include accuracy, macro and weighted $F_1$, and per-class $F_1$; AUROC/AUPRC for binary tasks; and separate top-level and superfamily scores for hierarchical models. Per-superfamily $F_1$ is reported only for superfamilies with $\geq$30 test samples. For the external benchmark, FASTA headers are mapped to the model vocabulary by exact superfamily match, parent-class fallback, or exclusion; sequences outside the three-way head (SINE, RC/Helitron, Unknown) are excluded.

\subsection{Implementation}\label{ssec:implementation}

All models were implemented in PyTorch 2.0+ and trained either locally on Apple-silicon MPS or on NVIDIA A100 GPU on CSD3 (Cambridge Service for Data Driven Discovery). Training and inference use full-precision FP32; validation losses, validation macro $F_1$ and the learned attention gate weights are logged each epoch and used to drive early stopping. The hyperparameter values used for all models are summarised in Table~\ref{tab:hyperparams}.

\section{Results}\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Terminal Inverted Repeat Detection}\label{ssec:tir}

As a proof of concept, the v1 CNN (Section~\ref{ssec:methods_v1}) was trained for binary TIR-presence detection. Of the 145{,}000 Pantera sequences, approximately 11{,}500 carry a TIR annotation; after stratified subsampling to a balanced training split, the model achieves 86\% accuracy and macro $F_1 = 0.86$ (TIR-positive $F_1 = 0.85$, TIR-negative $F_1 = 0.86$). Balanced subsampling converged more stably than loss-based reweightings (focal loss, inverse-frequency and inverse-square-root weighting), which introduced oscillating validation $F_1$. An auxiliary regression head on the pooled embedding recovers sequence length well ($r \approx 0.94$) but cannot predict absolute canvas position ($r \approx 0.07$--$0.18$): the masked global average pool is position-invariant by construction, so absolute placement information is unavailable. Section~\ref{ssec:limitations} outlines an architectural fix.


\subsection{Hierarchical DNA Transposon Classification (v3)}\label{ssec:hier}

The v3 hierarchical CNN classifier (Section~\ref{ssec:methods_v3}) extends the TIR-detection model to the DNA transposon subclassification task. It is trained on 20{,}000 subsampled non-DNA sequences and 30{,}431 DNA-transposon sequences across the seven DNA superfamilies with sufficient sample size, with 6{,}141 sequences held out for testing. 

\subsubsection{Performance}

The hierarchical model achieves strong performance on both tasks. For binary classification (DNA transposon vs other TE classes), the model reaches 91.3\% accuracy with a macro $F_1$ of 0.87. For superfamily classification among DNA transposons, accuracy is 94.4\% with a macro $F_1$ of 0.90.

Performance varies broadly with training support (Table~\ref{tab:v3_sf_f1}). DNA/hAT achieves the highest $F_1$ (0.97) and DNA/CMC the lowest (0.68, $n=39$ test sequences). The main driver of CMC's low precision (0.55) is confusion with the generic \textit{DNA} label: sequences annotated only at class level share compositional features with CMC elements and are frequently misassigned there. All other superfamilies exceed $F_1 = 0.93$.

\subsection{Hybrid CNN and K-mer Graph Neural Network Model (v4)}\label{ssec:hybrid}

The v4 hybrid model (Section~\ref{ssec:methods_v4}) augments the CNN tower with a parallel windowed $k$-mer GNN branch and fuses the two embeddings through a learned attention gate, aiming to capture positional and compositional information. It is evaluated first in the same binary (DNA vs non-DNA) setting as v3, then extended to a three-class head in v4.2 / v4.3 (Section~\ref{ssec:fullset}). 

In the binary setting, the learned gate weights remain highly variable throughout training and no branch consistently dominates (Figure~\ref{fig:v4_training}, lower-left panel), suggesting that the model does not prefer a fixed CNN-vs-GNN split for the binary task. Gate weight behaviour differs markedly in the three-class extension: see Figure~\ref{fig:gate_weights} and Section~\ref{ssec:hybrid_disc}. 

\subsubsection{Comparison with the CNN-Only Model}

The hybrid model improves on the CNN-only baseline on both tasks (Table~\ref{tab:v3_v4_sf_f1}; Figure~\ref{fig:v4_confusion}). Binary accuracy rises from 91.3\,\% to 93.1\,\% (macro $F_1$ 0.87\,$\to$\,0.89). A standalone $k$-mer GNN achieves AUROC\,=\,0.905 for binary DNA detection but only 41\,\% balanced accuracy across individual classes, confirming that global composition encodes class-level but not superfamily-level information (Section~\ref{ssec:fullset}).

\subsection{Multi-Class Extension: Three-Class Classification (v4.2 and v4.3)}\label{ssec:fullset}

The hybrid model is extended to a three-class head distinguishing DNA, LTR, and LINE: v4.2 trained on the full VGP dataset and v4.3 with three species withheld for external validation.

\subsubsection{Three-Class Reformulation}

Rare or ambiguous classes (SINE: 0.42\%, PLE: 0.20\%, RC: 0.12\%) and unannotated sequences are excluded; 50{,}431 sequences across 23 superfamilies remain. Each superfamily is capped at 3{,}000 sequences (effective training set: 30{,}727). The CNN+GNN architecture is unchanged; only the top-level head is broadened to three outputs.

\subsubsection{Overfitting Analysis in v4.2}

Table~\ref{tab:v4_2_splits} shows the overfitting clearly: validation SF $F_1$ reaches 1.000 while test SF $F_1$ is only 0.872 (gap 0.127), whereas the top-level class head generalises well (gap 0.016). The superfamily gap likely reflects memorisation of genome-specific subvariants in rare superfamilies ($n < 500$ training samples).

The 65-epoch training trajectory (Figure~\ref{fig:v4_2_training}) shows validation class and SF $F_1$ climbing monotonically to near-perfect values ($>0.99$) while the held-out test SF $F_1$ is only 0.872 (Table~\ref{tab:v4_2_splits}) --- the signature of fitting a compositionally similar validation split.

\subsubsection{Performance (v4.3)}

At the superfamily level, accuracy is 89.3\% and macro $F_1$ is 0.839 across 23 superfamilies. LINE subfamilies score highest (CR1 $F_1 = 0.992$, RTE $= 0.985$, L2 $= 0.965$), reflecting their distinctive compositional profiles. LTR superfamilies are mixed; the generic LTR label (conflating multiple lineages) is the most challenging ($F_1 = 0.714$). DNA/CMC ($F_1 = 0.494$, $n = 36$) and LINE/L1-Tx1 ($F_1 = 0.549$, $n = 69$) have the lowest scores, attributable to very limited test support. All 15 most common misclassifications occur within the same top-level class, confirming that the model reliably identifies the transposition mechanism even when the precise superfamily is ambiguous.

\subsubsection{K-mer Features Separate Top-Level Classes}

Analysis of k-mer features reveals that compositional patterns alone largely distinguish the three major TE classes. Five-fold cross-validation on 7-mer frequencies gives a logistic regression macro $F_1$ of $0.855 \pm 0.010$ and a random forest macro $F_1$ of $0.848 \pm 0.015$, both substantially above the majority-class baseline (Figure~\ref{fig:kmer_separation}). This strong baseline separability confirms the motivation for the GNN component of the hybrid architecture stated in Section~\ref{ssec:gnn_intro}: compositional signals provide strong priors for top-level class identity, and the attention gate can lean on this modality when local motifs are ambiguous.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{When Does the GNN Branch Help?}\label{ssec:hybrid_disc}

The GNN contributes a clear improvement on the class-level task (binary v4, three-way v4.3) but not on DNA-superfamily classification, which is essentially indistinguishable between the hybrid v4 and the pure CNN v3. In the three-class model the gate converges to GNN weight $\sim$0.72 from $\sim$epoch 25 (Figure~\ref{fig:gate_weights}), while in binary v4 it remains highly variable (Section~\ref{ssec:hybrid}). This is consistent with the $k$-mer logistic regression already recovering most of the class-level structure (Section~\ref{ssec:fullset}): the GNN sees global composition most clearly, which is informative for top-level mechanism but not for within-class motif differences. The attention gate is best read as a soft prior on which modality to trust, not as a generic improvement on top of the CNN.

A procedural note: late RC fusion (averaging CNN embeddings from both strands before the head) proved simpler and equally accurate compared to an earlier variant that symmetrised inputs after the first convolutional layer, likely because early fusion constrains the filters to be RC-symmetric and restricts representable motifs.

\subsection{Generalisation and Domain Shift}

The contrast between within-VGP accuracy (98.2\%) and the three held-out species (overall 55.0\%; zebra finch 33.3\%) demonstrates substantial lineage-dependence across the amniote clades sampled here. LTR discrimination fails most severely (F1~=~0.21--0.50 across all three species), with avian-specific ERV families the main driver; DNA classification also collapses in zebra finch (F1~=~0.35) but is partially preserved in platypus (F1~=~0.81) and alligator (F1~=~0.70), reflecting partial training-set overlap for mammalian and reptilian DNA transposons.

This is a distribution constraint, not an architectural deficiency: the encoder transfers reasonably to platypus, whose TE landscape is closer to the mammalian and reptilian training set. Broader phylogenetic coverage or domain-adversarial training~\cite{ganin2015unsupervised} are the natural remedies.

\subsection{Overfitting Patterns Across the Hierarchy}

A consistent observation across v3, v4 and especially v4.2 is that superfamily heads overfit substantially more than top-level class heads on the same training run. In v4.2 the superfamily test gap reached 0.127 while the three-class gap was only 0.016. The asymmetry is again consistent with the broader picture: top-level classes are large, well-supported and biologically distinct, so their decision boundary is constrained by many examples; superfamilies are smaller, internally heterogeneous and partly defined by alignment to reference libraries, so the model has both the capacity and the incentive to memorise genome-specific subvariants.

The mitigations adopted in v4.3 (early stopping at 40 epochs, label smoothing, species hold-out) reduced but did not eliminate the gap; the residual is concentrated in the rarest superfamilies ($n < 100$ test sequences). Higher minimum-sample thresholds and hierarchical loss weighting are the most direct remedies.

\subsection{Interpretability: Saliency and Unsupervised Structure}

Two interpretability analyses complement the supervised results. Saliency (Figure~\ref{fig:saliency_analysis}) shows qualitatively that within-class confusions carry overlapping attribution envelopes (shared motifs), while confident across-class errors show diffuse saliency; these observations are limited to four cases and remain hypothesis-generating. Semi-supervised contrastive clustering (v5) recovers top-level class separation in the UMAP projection; the 252 Ward subclusters have modest but non-zero correspondence to annotated superfamilies (ARI~=~0.036, homogeneity~=~0.247), with mixed and Unknown-dominant clusters representing candidate targets for expert curation.

\subsection{Limitations and Future Directions}\label{ssec:limitations}

A methodological limitation of the within-distribution results should be noted explicitly: all v1--v4.3 test sets are split at the sequence level with class-balancing applied before splitting, so the test distribution is artificially balanced and test $F_1$ is inflated for rare classes. The external benchmark (Section~\ref{ssec:mini_benchmark}), evaluated on unmodified curated libraries, provides the more conservative performance estimate and should be treated as the primary evidence of generalisation.

The most concrete architectural limitation is the boundary-prediction head: because it reads from the masked global average pool, which is position-invariant by construction, absolute canvas position is unrecoverable ($r \approx 0.07$--$0.18$) despite accurate length prediction ($r \approx 0.94$). An in-progress v4.4 variant adds sinusoidal positional channels to the input and replaces the regression head with a per-position segmentation head; if it improves localisation without harming classification, it is the natural successor to v4.3.

Beyond v4.4, three directions are natural: (i) broader phylogenetic training coverage or domain-adversarial learning~\cite{ganin2015unsupervised} to close the cross-species gap; (ii) transposase and reverse-transcriptase domain features to disambiguate superfamilies sharing nucleotide composition; and (iii) DNA-sequence foundation models pre-trained at scale and fine-tuned on Pantera.
